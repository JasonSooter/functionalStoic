# Types of Fallacy

## Summary

A fallacy is a flaw or error in reasoning that weakens an argument. They are often subtle and can be persuasive, leading people to accept false conclusions. Understanding different types of fallacies is crucial for evaluating the strength of arguments.

## Common Types of Fallacies

1. **Arrival:** This fallacy occurs when people believe that achieving big objectives will give them a lot of happiness, and then are bitterly disappointed to find that doing so is a letdown. After a big achievement, many people experience depression. True satisfaction comes from progress in the struggle toward the goal. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01j5pyv4qm5xwt7c4mk7rgbvqc))
	- #people/authors/arthur-c-brooks discussed this in [Fyodor Dostoyevsky’s Formula for Happiness](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2024/07/fyodor-dostoyevsky-formula-happiness/679203/)
2. **Ad Hominem**: This fallacy occurs when someone attacks the character or motive of a person making an argument rather than addressing the substance of the argument itself.
3. [[#Tu Quoque Fallacy]]
4. **Straw Man**: This involves misrepresenting or exaggerating someone's argument to make it easier to attack, rather than engaging with the actual points made.
5. **Appeal to Ignorance (Argumentum ad Ignorantiam)**: This fallacy asserts that a proposition is true because it has not been proven false, or vice versa.
6. **False Dilemma (Either/Or Fallacy)**: This fallacy presents only two options or sides when there are more available, forcing a choice between them as if they were the only possibilities.
7. **Slippery Slope**: It suggests that a relatively small first step will inevitably lead to a chain of related events culminating in some significant impact, often negative.
8. **Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question)**: This involves making a claim and then trying to support it with another statement that essentially repeats the original assertion without providing any new evidence.
9. **Hasty Generalization**: Making a broad generalization based on insufficient evidence, such as drawing conclusions from too small a sample size.
10. **Red Herring**: Introducing an irrelevant topic into an argument to divert attention from the original issue.
11. **Bandwagon Appeal (Ad Populum)**: Suggesting that something is true or right because everyone else believes it or does it.
12. **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc**: Assuming that because one event followed another, it was also caused by it; confusing correlation with causation.
13. [[#The Collector's Fallacy]]
14. [Narrative Fallacy](https://fs.blog/narrative-fallacy/) ^47eba9
	- Human beings have been appropriately called “the storytelling animal” because of our instinct to construct and seek meaning in narrative. It’s likely that long before we developed the ability to write or to create objects, we were telling stories and thinking in stories. Nearly all social organizations, from religious institutions to corporations to nation-states, run on constructions of the narrative instinct.

## Importance of Recognizing Fallacies

Recognizing fallacies helps individuals critically evaluate arguments they encounter in everyday life and avoid being misled by faulty reasoning. It also aids in constructing stronger arguments and engaging in more productive discussions by focusing on logical coherence rather than emotional persuasion or rhetorical tricks.

### The Collectors Fallacy

- [source](https://zettelkasten.de/posts/collectors-fallacy/)

There’s a tendency in all of us to gather useful stuff and feel good about it. I've fallen for this feeling many times in the past. Honestly, the truth cuts straight to the bone. Examples include:
- the books that I've purchased
- How I collect knowledge, slack threads, blog posts, docs, etc. at work

In some ways, to collect is a reward in itself. It can feel as though something is being accomplished when collecting so much. As knowledge workers, we’re inclined to look for the next groundbreaking thought, for intellectual stimulation. In the process, we pile up promising books and articles, and we store half the internet as bookmarks, just so we get the feeling of being on the cutting edge.

> I guess I'm glad that I'm not alone, but 😞

#### Why is This a Fallacy?

Because ‘to know about something’ isn’t the same as ‘knowing something’.

While his is very true. The only point of distinction that I'd make is that at work, knowing where something is or at least how to find it does indeed come in very handy for helping to direct others. But, I'd sure rather "know" the thing as well.

Just knowing about a thing is less than superficial, knowing about is merely to be certain of its existence, nothing more. This is very true, but as mentioned above, I do find that it is useful at work for directing others to resources that may help them move forward. However, I think in the end, I find it quite hollow not knowing about the thing.

Ultimately, this fake-knowledge is hindering us on our road to true excellence. To some extent, this will come in time and it also this seems very closely related to #concepts/impostor-syndrome. Often, impostor syndrome creates feelings of fake-knowledge. So it is likely important to keep that in mind.

> [!quote] Until we merge the contents, the information, ideas, and thoughts of other people _**INTO OUR OWN KNOWLEDGE**_, we haven’t really learned a thing.

#### Collectors Don't Make Progress

The worst we could do is to pile up copies until the stack grows intimidatingly high, until it becomes unmanageable.

I've found this to be true, with my stack of books. The tall stack of books that Twilio has paid for is great, but I've read almost none of them. It's definitely at an unmanageable point. ## Tu Quoque Fallacy

**Tu quoque** (Latin for _“you too”_) is a **type of ad hominem fallacy** that dismisses a claim by accusing the speaker of hypocrisy.
It argues that because someone fails to act according to their own advice, their argument must be false — which is logically invalid.

An example:

> Person A: "You shouldn’t lie."
> Person B: "But you lied yesterday!"

This response **dodges the claim** rather than addressing its truth.

### Core Problem

The tu quoque fallacy **confuses personal inconsistency with logical inconsistency**.
An argument’s validity doesn’t depend on whether the person making it consistently follows it.

### Why It Persuades

- Appeals to **fairness** (“practice what you preach”)
- Shifts focus from the issue to the person
- Creates **moral distraction** instead of rational rebuttal

### Common Forms

1. **Health advice:**
	 “You smoke, so your argument that smoking is unhealthy doesn’t count.”

2. **Environmental ethics:**
	 “You drive a car, so you can’t criticize pollution.”

3. **Politics:**
	 “You accuse my candidate of corruption, but yours did it too!”

### How to Respond

- **Acknowledge** inconsistency if relevant:
	“You’re right, I haven’t always done that — but the argument still stands.”
- **Re-focus** on evidence:
	“Let’s look at whether the claim itself is true.”

---

### Related Fallacies

- **Ad Hominem:** Attacking the person instead of the argument.
- **Red Herring:** Distracting from the issue with irrelevant points.
- **Whataboutism:** A modern political form of _tu quoque_.

---

### Summary

Tu quoque undermines honest debate by turning rational discussion into moral comparison.
Consistency matters for credibility — but **truth doesn’t depend on who says it.**
