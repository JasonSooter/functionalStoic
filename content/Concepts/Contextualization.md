# Contextualization

> Seeing Problems for What They *Really* Are

> [!quote] It may be easier to see solutions if you can distinguish between **context** and **content**. If you can place a problem within the framework of the larger universe its dimensions are put into perspective and automatically diminished.
> —Peter Cundill [on the power of perspective](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jr6tb5ytrj154zvmp67bwzjd)

## Summary

Contextualization refers to the process of placing information, events, thoughts, or actions within the larger framework of circumstances that surround them to gain a better understanding of their meaning and implications. This concept is used across various fields such as education, history, linguistics, and theology.

In education, contextualization involves relating new information to students' existing knowledge and experiences, helping them understand and retain new concepts more effectively. In history, it involves examining events within the broader socio-political and cultural settings, allowing a more nuanced understanding of historical incidents.

In linguistics, contextualization helps interpret language use based on situational context, including factors like the speaker's intent, the relationship between participants, and the cultural background.

In theology and missiology, contextualization involves adapting religious messages to different cultural contexts, ensuring they resonate more effectively with diverse audiences while maintaining core tenets.

Overall, contextualization is essential for meaningful comprehension and communication, as it provides the background necessary to interpret information accurately.

## Content

### Why This Matters

Most problems feel overwhelming because we experience them **from the inside**. Contextualization flips that view: step outside the content, locate it in its natural system, and watch its apparent size—and stuck-ness—shrink.

### Content Vs Context

| Term        | What it is                                                                                 | Typical Blind Spot                |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------- |
| **Content** | Immediate facts, numbers, emotions, deadlines—the *thing* screaming for attention.         | We assume it’s the whole reality. |
| **Context** | The larger system: timelines, incentives, constraints, history, stakeholders, environment. | We forget it even exists.         |

### Why We Confuse Them

1. **Cognitive load** – The brain defaults to the loudest signal.
2. **Urgency theater – Modern workflows reward firefighting.
3. **Ego attachment** – “My problem” feels personal, not systemic.

---

### The Contextualization Move (mini-framework)

![[The Contextualization Move Framework.png]]

1. **Name** the content: one-sentence problem statement.
2. **Locate** the nearest *containing system* (team, market, family, decade, universe—scale as needed).
3. **Map** two or three forces in that system affecting the content.
4. **Re-rate** the problem’s size *relative to those forces*.
5. **Surface** at least one option now visible only at the larger scale.

> *Tip ✏️ Sketch concentric circles—problem at center, systems radiating outward.*

---

### 5 · Quick Illustration

> **Problem (content)**: “Our release is late; customers will churn.”
> **Contextualize**:
> *System*: SaaS lifecycle + B2B renewal rhythms
> *Forces*: grace-period norms, competitor timelines, customer-success buffers
> **Shifted Perspective**: a three-week slip ≠ existential threat; a targeted comms plan + feature-flag rollout preserves trust. New solution appears: staged release with opt-in beta.

---

### 6 · Tactics to Build the Muscle

- **Timeline throw** – Ask: *Will this matter in 6 months? 6 years?*
- **Stakeholder zoom** – Who is *two hops away* and how do they see it?
- **Constraint flip** – Treat every “hard limit” as a variable; ask what could change it.
- **Historical echo** – Has a past version of this problem been solved elsewhere? Borrow context, not content.

---

### 7 · When Contextualization Fails

- **Scope creep** – Zooming out so far that actionability dissolves.
- **Analysis-paralysis** – Using “more context” as procrastination.

*Mitigation*: set a timer; once new options appear, zoom back in and ship.

---

### 8 · Closing Thought

Contextualization isn’t distancing yourself from responsibility; it’s **re-anchoring** responsibility in reality. The moment you can say, “Ah, this is just *one tile* in a much bigger mosaic,” you’re free to pick the right tool—and that’s when solutions show up.
