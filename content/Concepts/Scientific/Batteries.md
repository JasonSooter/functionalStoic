# Batteries

## Summary

Batteries are devices that store and release electrical energy through chemical reactions. They are made up of one or more electrochemical cells. Each cell contains a positive electrode (cathode), a negative electrode (anode), and an electrolyte that facilitates the movement of ions between the electrodes.

The operation of a battery involves a redox reaction where oxidation occurs at the anode, releasing electrons, and reduction occurs at the cathode, accepting electrons. The movement of electrons from the anode to the cathode through an external circuit provides electrical power.

Batteries are classified into two main types: primary (non-rechargeable) and secondary (rechargeable). Primary batteries, like alkaline batteries, are used until depleted and then disposed of. Secondary batteries, like lithium-ion and nickel-cadmium batteries, can be recharged and used multiple times.

Batteries vary in size, capacity, and application. They are used in consumer electronics, vehicles, industrial machines, and as backup power sources. Recent advancements focus on improving energy density, longevity, charging speed, and environmental impact, with innovations like solid-state batteries and new chemistries.

The demand for efficient energy storage has increased with the growth of renewable energy and electric vehicles, making batteries a critical component in modern technology and sustainability efforts.
