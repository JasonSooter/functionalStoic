## The Up and Comers Are the Ones Who Wrote Down the Plan

> [!quote] The person who writes down the thing has tremendous power. One of the ways you find the up-and-comers at a tech company is by seeing who wrote down the plan
> —Marc Andreessen ([source](https://x.com/david_perell/status/1709569520749134043?s=20))

This simple fact stood out to me because it's an easy way to stand out as an engineer.

There are huge benefits to doing it, e.g.:
- clearer thinking
- building alignment
- increasing your visibility

Yet few engineers do it. It's often seen as overhead that isn't as enjoyable as writing code.

Their reluctance can be your opportunity.

# Writing is the Most Useful Skill for a Career in Tech

Doing it well in industry is different from what you learned in school.

Give me two minutes to show you how to write effectively at work:

1. Make the first line interesting

If you want someone to read your writing, show them why they should care immediately. It’s like a “hook” (but much less sensational in the work context).

1. Write at <7th grade reading level

Your ideas will be better understood if you use simpler language. Just because your audience can read technical writing doesn’t mean they want to.

I use Hemmingway (not sponsored) to check my writing before sharing it.

1. Use summaries

No matter how good your writing is, people’s attention will still fall off as they read.

Put what your audience must know in a few lines at the top of the post. I even make this explicit by labeling it with “tl;dr” (“too long; didn’t read”).

1. Optimize for skimming

Help your audience find information relevant to them (instead of skipping over your writing entirely) by using:

- Section headings
- Index w/anchor links (for longer posts)
- Lists
- Bold text for main points
- Shorter paragraphs

1. Make your ask clear

If you’re writing to ask your audience to take action, don’t bury your ask somewhere in the middle of your message. Share it right at the beginning or end (if they need context to understand your ask).

- source: Ryan Peterman

## Tips for Amazon Writers

1. **Use less than 30 words per sentence**  
   - Replace: *Due to the fact that* → *because*  
   - Replace: *Totally lacked the ability to* → *could not*
2. **Replace adjectives with data**  
   - Replace: *We made the performance much faster* →  *We reduced server-side tp90 latency from 10 ms to 1 ms.*
3. **Eliminate weasel words**  
   - Replace: *nearly all customers* → *87% of Prime members*  
   - Replace: *significantly better* → *+25 basis points (bps)*
4. **Does your writing pass the "So what" test?**
5. **If you get a question, reply with one of the four Amazon answers:**
   1. *Yes.*  
   2. *No.*  
   3. *A number.*  
   4. *I don’t know (and will follow up when I do).*

## Amazon Writing Style Tip #2

**Be objective: Avoid adjectives and adverbs**
- **Adjectives are imprecise** and don’t contribute to making a decision.
- **Most Amazonians react negatively** to buzzwords and qualifications without data.

### Examples

| **Subjective**                                                         | **Better** | **Objective**                                                                                   |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Sales increased significantly in Q4, due to use of holiday promotions. |            | Unit sales increased **by 40%** in Q4 2011, compared to Q4 2010, because of holiday promotions. |
| We made the application much faster.                                   |            | We reduced server-side trailing 90-day latency **from 10 ms to 1 ms.**                          |
| This will make the endeavor extremely successful.                      |            | This will increase output **by 2.5%.**                                                          |

## Amazon Writing Style Tip #3

- **Avoid jargon and acronyms** as they exclude non-experts and newcomers.
- **Always explain technical terms, acronyms, and abbreviations** the first time they appear.
	- **Example:** *After we sign the Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)…*
- **Weasel words are vague** and create the impression of meaning. Don’t use them!
	- *“would help the solution”*
	- *“might bring clarity”*
	- *“should result in benefits”*
	- *“significantly better”*
	- *“arguably the best”*

One simple habit that helped me reach Staff Engineer (IC6) was sharing my work.

Every time I completed a project, I shared my results using the same rough format:

- TLDR: 1 sentence explaining the launch and calling out the most impactful result
- Context: 2-3 sentences explaining the problem & solution
- Results: 2-4 bullet points of the detailed benefits of this launch (e.g. top metric movements, user benefits, cost savings, etc)
- Next Steps: 2-3 sentences explaining the rollout timeline and follow-up work
- Tags & Thanks: mention all relevant peers, managers and tech leads

By making my work visible, I built a reputation within my team. When it was time for promotions, my contributions were already well-known.
