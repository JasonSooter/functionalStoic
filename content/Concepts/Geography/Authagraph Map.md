# Authagraph Map

![[Authagraph.svg]]

## Summary

The Authagraph map is a world map projection designed to represent the globe more accurately than traditional maps. Developed by Japanese architect Hajime Narukawa in 1999, this projection aims to address the distortions present in conventional maps like the Mercator projection. The Authagraph map divides the globe into 96 triangles, projecting them onto a tetrahedron, and then unfolding this shape into a rectangle while preserving proportions.

## Key Features

- **Equal Area Projection**: Unlike many traditional maps, the Authagraph maintains equal area, meaning countries are depicted in true proportion to their actual size on Earth.
- **Minimal Distortion**: This map minimizes distortion of shapes and angles more effectively than other rectangular maps.
- **Versatility**: The modular design allows for different orientations and configurations without losing accuracy, making it suitable for various applications.

## Applications

- **Education**: Provides a more accurate representation of the Earth for educational purposes.
- **Media and Art**: Suitable for artistic and media projects requiring an accurate depiction of global [[geography]].
- **Navigation and Cartography**: Offers a new perspective that can be beneficial for navigation tools and cartographic studies.

Overall, the Authagraph map presents a significant advancement in cartography by offering a more balanced view of our planet, challenging conventional perceptions shaped by less accurate projections.
