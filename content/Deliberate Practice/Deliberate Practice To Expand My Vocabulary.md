# Deliberate Practice To Expand My Vocabulary

## Summary

Deliberate practice is a focused and systematic approach to improving a specific skill, such as expanding your vocabulary. It involves breaking down the learning process into smaller parts, identifying weaknesses, testing new strategies, and integrating these learnings into your overall practice. The key components include sustained effort, concentration, and a commitment to lifelong learning.

For vocabulary expansion, deliberate practice might include:

1. **Identifying New Words**: Actively seek out new words from various sources like books, articles, and conversations.
2. **Understanding and Using Words**: Look up definitions, understand the context in which the words are used, and practice using them in sentences.
3. **Review and Reinforcement**: Regularly review the new words and their meanings to reinforce your memory.
4. **Feedback and Adjustment**: Seek feedback on your usage of new words and adjust your learning strategies accordingly.

Examples from your notes include learning words like "Verboten" (meaning forbidden or prohibited) and "Succor" (assistance and support in times of hardship). By incorporating these steps into your routine, you can systematically expand your vocabulary and improve your language skills.

### Vouchsafed

**Pronunciation:** /vaʊtʃˈseɪft/ - vowch-SAYFT
**Part of speech:** verb (past tense/past participle)
**Definition:** to grant or bestow something as a favor or privilege, typically in a formal or respectful manner
**Etymology:** From Middle English "vouchsafe," derived from two elements:
- **Vouch**: From Old French "vouchier" (to call/summon), from Latin "vocare" (to call)
- **Safe**: From Old English "sæf" (to grant/give)

**Example:** "He vouchsafed no answer" - suggests the person did not grant the speaker the favor of a response, carrying a sense of formality in withholding an answer.

**Source:** Herman Melville's "Bartleby, the Scrivener: A Story of Wall-Street"

_content_
- While starting a conversation with `Hi, How are you?` is a common occurrence, this does not mean it doesn't have subjective & negative downsides. This is where Emotional Intelligence comes in!

>Nearly every time we open a conversation like this, however, we're starting out with an agreement to avoid the truth. It's a social convention in which everybody knows that what we say and what we mean are very different things.
>You understand instinctively. Most of the time, you don't ask "How are you?" because you actually want the other person to answer your question seriously.
>Instead, you want them to say the expected thing: "Fine, thanks, how are you?" so that you can proceed to whatever else is really on your mind.

- Powerful truth here, but instead of avoiding these types of conversations. I need to rise to the occasion
- If I do say it, I need to ensure it comes from an `other-centeredness` perspective.

>Why send an unintentional, somewhat negative message if you don't have to?

- Sometimes, cutting to the chase makes sense. But striving for better should be the goal when at all possible.

>First, people who simply ask more questions, and follow-up questions -- who go beyond "How are you?" for example -- were "better liked by their conversation partners."

- Key finding to keep in mind. Simply getting to additional questions (even if started with "How are you?") is likely to produce benefit in the future with these people.

>Second, conversation partners who were told to ask more questions were perceived as being "higher in responsiveness, [which is] an interpersonal construct that captures listening, understanding, validation, and care."

- Ask more questions. This is a tip to remember! Just ask more questions!

>right now, given the pandemic and recession. For so many, the truthful answer to "How are you?" isn't what they wish it to be -- and maybe not what they'd like to dwell on at the moment.

- Use of this could

>To ask 'How are you?' is either to make the conversation very gloomy, very fast or to force someone to lie straight to your face and say they're fine. We need better questions to ask.

- Wow, So true! I should not put people in the awkward position of forcing them to lie straight to my face. If I know this (which I do), I can no longer condone doing it.

> In those cases, the key is to follow-up, and then follow-up again.

- When valid opportunities arise to say "How Are You", it is fine to ask it. However, the proof that it was warranted is in the "Follow-up Asks". Did I really learn how they are doing?

> "Jennifer, before we begin, how was Ryan's first day of virtual kindergarten yesterday?" As opposed to, simply: "How was everyone's weekend--good?"

- Such a great example, one implies I've both considered their situation when asking, along with care about the response to a specific question. Either way, I understand more clearly how they are, yet the expectation is they will feel I actually cared how they were?

>If you're using "How are you?" as a conversation-starter, but, in truth, you're dreading the idea that anyone might take you seriously and launch into an expansive, truthful answer, the best advice is to stick instead with a positive declaration.
>Not a question, but instead something like:
>- "I'm happy to see you."
>- "Thank you for being here--let's get started."
>- "I hope you had a nice weekend," or "I hope your family is doing well."
>- Or just a plain, simple: "Good morning."

- The key to this technique is that while not "Other Centered", they aren't "Self-Centered" either. They are more true to most situations (at least work situations) where most reasonable co-workers don't expect me to care about their specific situation that day.

>(By the way, if you're on the receiving end of "How are you?" or even "I hope your family is doing well," and you honestly don't want to answer in great detail, the easy, polite way out is just to say "Thank you for asking" without offering any other details.)

- This is good to look out for. Also a good response on my end. "Thank you for asking!" is a perfectly acceptable response.

>Also, don't make inauthentic conversation-starter questions even worse by suggesting the answer in the question itself, as in the "How was your weekend--good?" question above and similar things, like: "Everybody has what they need, right?"

- This applies to all situations. Do not ask a question that I do not expect to be answered by providing the answer in the question
