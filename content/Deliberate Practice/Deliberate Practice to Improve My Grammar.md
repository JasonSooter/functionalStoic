# Deliberate Practice to Improve My Grammar

- [Em Dash vs En Dash](https://languagetool.org/insights/post/dashes/)

## e.g. Vs. i.e.

- **e.g.** stands for _exempli gratia_ in Latin, meaning "for example." Use it when you want to give examples to illustrate what you're talking about without listing everything.
- **i.e.** is short for _id est_, meaning "that is" or "in other words." Use it to clarify or rephrase your previous statement for better understanding.

So, **e.g.** introduces examples, while **i.e.** provides clarification.

Some examples for each:

For e.g.:
1. "You might try a low-impact exercise, e.g., walking or swimming, to stay active without straining your joints."
2. "There are many scripting languages available, e.g., Python, Ruby, and PHP, that can help with your development tasks."
3. "We should focus on renewable energy sources, e.g., solar, wind, and hydroelectric power, to reduce carbon emissions."
4. "Many fruits are rich in vitamins, e.g., oranges for vitamin C, bananas for potassium, and avocados for vitamin E."
5. "Some common data structures, e.g., arrays, linked lists, and hash tables, are crucial for efficient programming."

**For i.e.:**
1. "We need to refactor the code to make it DRY, i.e., Don't Repeat Yourself, which means consolidating redundant code into reusable functions."
2. "The project's MVP, i.e., Minimum Viable Product, should include the core features that allow it to be deployed and tested by early users."
3. "He's a full-stack developer, i.e., he can handle both front-end and back-end development tasks."
4. "To optimize performance, consider using a CDN, i.e., Content Delivery Network, which caches content at various locations closer to end-users."
5. "Let's implement an FP approach, i.e., Functional Programming, focusing on pure functions and avoiding shared state, mutable data, and side-effects."

# Homophones, Homographs, and Homonyms

## Homophones

- words that sound the same, but have different means.
- They can be spelled differently or the same.
- Greek root meaning:
	- Homos: same
	- Phone: sound
- A few examples:
	- bark
		- (noun): cry that comes from some animals or the outer layer of a tree
		- (verb): to make the sound of a bark or give orders
	- plain
		- (noun) an extensive area of flat, tree-less land
		- (adjective) unadorned, not decorated, or simple
- Examples that are spelled differently:
	- they're, their, there
	- to, too, two
	- it's, it's
	- tail, tale
	- bye, by, but

## Homographs

- Words that are spelled the same, but differ in meaning for pronunciation
- Greek meaning of graph is writing
- Examples:
	- minute
		- sixty seconds
		- extremely small
	- tear
		- drop of liquid from the eye
		- to rip by pulling apart
	- lead
		- chemical element
		- to go in front and guide in the right direction

## Homonyms

- Some consider Homonyms an umbrella category for both [[#Homographs]] & [[#Homophones]]
- Some consider [[#Homonyms]] to be the subset of words that are BOTH [[#Homophones]] & [[#Homographs]]

## Transitive and Intransitive Verbs

Understanding the difference between transitive and intransitive verbs is crucial for constructing grammatically correct sentences. Here's a brief overview:

### Transitive Verbs

- **Definition**: Transitive verbs are action verbs that require one or more objects to complete their meaning.
- **Characteristics**: They answer the question "what?" or "whom?" after the verb.
- **Examples**:
	- "She **buys** groceries every Sunday."
		- (Buys what? Groceries)
	- "He **read** the book in one sitting."
		- (Read what? The book)

### Intransitive Verbs

- **Definition**: Intransitive verbs are action verbs that do not require an object to complete their meaning.
- **Characteristics**: They make sense without needing to answer "what?" or "whom?"
- **Examples**:
	- "The baby **cried** loudly."
		- (No object is necessary)
	- "They **arrived** late to the party."
		- (No object is necessary)

### Practice Exercise

Try identifying whether the verb in each sentence is transitive or intransitive:

1. "The cat slept peacefully on the couch."
2. "Can you pass the salt, please?"
3. "The students laughed at his joke."
4. "She sang beautifully during the concert."

By regularly reviewing and practicing these concepts, you'll strengthen your understanding of grammar and improve your writing skills over time.
