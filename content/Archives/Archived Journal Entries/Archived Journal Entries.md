# Archives

## Summary

The term "archives" refers to collections of historical documents or records that are preserved for their enduring value, often because they provide information about a particular institution, location, people, or event. Archives can be physical or digital and are typically managed by archivists. These professionals are responsible for the acquisition, preservation, and organization of archival materials, ensuring that they are accessible for research and reference.

Archives are commonly associated with libraries, museums, government entities, corporations, and universities. They contain a wide range of materials, including letters, manuscripts, photographs, maps, films, and digital files. The primary purpose of an archive is to serve as a repository of knowledge and cultural heritage. This helps researchers, historians, and the general public explore and analyze the past.

The management of archives involves several key processes, such as appraisal (determining which records have lasting value), arranging and describing records to make them searchable, and ensuring their preservation against physical and digital decay. Digital archives have become increasingly important, as they allow for broader access and easier sharing of information across the globe.

Overall, archives play a crucial role in safeguarding historical narratives, supporting accountability and transparency, and fostering an understanding of societal developments.
