## Thomas Carlson's Travel Tips

- #people/friends/thomas-carlson

I wrote these travel tips a few years ago, but I think they've all held up well.  Most of this you probably know, but maybe something sparks an idea for our Europe trip.

Rules for Traveling:

1. Split your meals. Or at least order less than you feel you want. You’re going to think you’re starving, but you’re not. Smaller portions will help you travel all day, keep you from having tummy issues, and are much cheaper. You also end up trying more things! Our expression, “you can always order more”. You can also leave food behind! Just because it’s free or on your plate doesn’t mean you have to eat it. Stick close to your regular diet for non “fun” or fancy meals, and the whole trip will go much better. Basically, skip the airport cheeseburger for a better one later. 
2. Snacks and water. Pack some granola bars and too much water, always. Most fights, and violating rule 1, are caused because one person is hungry. When you start to argue, get out the snacks. Drinking lots of water too will help with hungry, head and stomach aches, and keep your energy up. Staying well hydrated also will keep your digestion system functioning – which is important and ideal if it’s functioning on your schedule.
	1. NOTE:, you may not want to drink the water everywhere. If that’s the case, don’t forget to avoid raw vegetables like lettuce, drinks with ice, and restaurant water glasses. 
3. Never miss an opportunity to use the bathroom. See one? Use it! Someone else needs to go? You go too! If you’re following rule #2, this should be no problem. Make sure you roll up a little TP and keep it in your purse or pocket. Bring hand sanitizer too and use early and often.
4. Protect your feet. Most travel trips, due to activity or cost, involve non-stop walking. If your legs hurt, joints are sore, or you get blisters, even the most beautiful view / attraction becomes a chore. Bring some moleskin or runners tape – if you feel a hot spot, don’t wait! Stop immediately and fix it. If you need new shoes, new arch support, or socks, buy them! Your trip cost at minimum ~$1200 per traveler, per 3-4 day trip and this might be your only vacation for 3–6 months. If that’s not worth $50 insoles, what is? Once your feet start hurting, there’s no going back. Don’t forget to stretch or rest as needed. Walking is great, but if you’re starting to hurt, pay for the cab.
5. Wear sunscreen. We make applying a face sunscreen part of our morning routine, even if we’re going to be “inside” all day – we usually aren’t. Bring Sunglasses with you everywhere. And always pack a swimsuit – you never know! 
6. Get rest. That sounds obvious, but really this means rest when you can – that’s a cab to or from the airport, on the train, and especially on a short connecting flight. Instead of reading or watching the movie, steal these moments for recharging, and you’ll be in much better shape to go out when you get where you’re going.
	- For an international flight, you want to try to calculate when you must sleep. If you take off at 8pm and have an 8-hour flight to Europe that lands at 10am, you MUST sleep on the flight. Typically, they do a meal service 1 hour in, so allow yourself one movie and then lights out! If you’re lucky, that’s 6 hours of bad sleep, and then it’s showtime! Make sure you stay awake until a normal time on the way there or home, or you’ll ruin your whole trip, being up at 2:45am and tired at 6pm.
7. ***Pack less than you think.***
	- It’s your vacation, so you would rather not be dirty or feeling less than “sleek”, but you’re better off planning a general idea of what you’ll wear for each day / activity and packing only that plus one or maybe two alternates. If you're moving around a lot, a hard to repack suitcase will force you to spend valuable time packing and repacking every day. Even if you’re not, fewer options will lead to less stress. Pick a shoe for the day and a shoe for the night. Wear your suit/sport coat on the plane if needed. And for the ladies – dresses are great! They don’t take up much space, and can be worn more than once. Our one splurge, we bring enough underwear to change twice a day.
8. ***Pack more medicine than you think.*** Once you have a good medicine bag made, it’s pretty easy to keep t full. ~$80-$100 at CVS/ Walgreens will get you enough of o make two or even three kits. Fill with IB prof, allergy, Imodium, tums, cough, sore throat, cold, Sudafed, Benadryl, and anything else that might come up unexpectedly. This kit is a lifesaver that will help you get ahead of something before it starts.
9. Get money before. If you run out, you can get more from an ATM. A local bank will give you a better exchange rate than the airport or train station. Make sure you call or go online to alert your Credit card you’re traveling. American Express has the best overseas rate, but it’s accepted almost no where in Europe, Africa, and South America. You can surprisingly use it in some places in Asia. Discover is similar – accepted in almost no countries. Visa and MasterCard are best for overseas travel. I like to get smaller bills, it’s easier to pay the cabs, restaurants, buy water, etc… safety most places isn’t an issue as long as you keep your wits, don’t waive a wad of cash around, and keep track of your belongings. A money belt isn’t necessary in Europe, and even in [[On China|China]] it only makes you feel safe in a big crowd, but otherwise not necessary if you don’t let people get really close. It’s also really hot. I always keep my passport and wallet in my front pocket.
10. Learn basic pleasantries. Hello, goodbye, thank you, excuse me, please. These few words will make a huge difference to your hosts, cab driver, and local shop/restaurant owner. It’s also fun. Your phone can be used for translating, but usually a hand gesture, a friendly smile, and being patient are better than using your phone.

> [!NOTE] Note
>  If you ask someone to take your picture, my rule of thumb is to find someone who doesn’t look faster than you are!

**Helpful Travel Tips**:

1. Phones:
	- Phones make the world small. You can now google map just about everywhere without asking local directions. You can use translate to translate signs by holding up your camera. Buy the international day pass from ATT or Verizon and for $10/day your phone uses your same plan from home. Most places have Wi-Fi – ask and get on when you can. If your phone plan offers free international data already, make a new friend — your “free” data will never work. Bring a portable USB charger or two and charge when you have downtime, so you can map later. Maps uses a ton of battery, and you’ll want your phone to take pictures. Our thought on pictures is leave the Fancy camera at home. You’re recording memories at 12mps on your phone, not shooting a car brochure. Unless you’re a photography buff and like the gear, it’s simpler, safer, and less hassle to shoot your trip pics on your phone.
	- In the states, the yelp app is great, but almost worthless overseas – we recommend TripAdvisor to find a good restaurants and bars.
2. Hotels, rental cars, and flight upgrades are a personal preference to be debated among travelers. Our advice – spend your time and money researching and buying what matters to you most.  For us, the flight is bad regardless of your seat, a fast, or fancy rental car is always fun, and the location of the hotel is more important than its amenities.  Although clean, comfortable, and private are a must with the hotel. 
3. Rental Cars tips:
	1. If you love cars, get a nice one! If you don’t, get the same brand you drive at home, so the controls are familiar. Always make sure the car rental place is “on site” or at the airport (unless all of them are offsite). The money you save on an off-site rental place isn’t worth it for the stress you’ll incur when it’s time to return your car, and you’re running late. For this reason, I also pre-pay for the gas, but that has more to do with how many miles you dive. I always decline the rental coverage because I’m booking with my credit card that has coverage. I enrolled in the Amex rental car coverage program, which charges an extra $20 per rental and gives me $100k vehicle coverage. If you want a fancier rental car, try using Priceline to get the best, most expensive “standard” or full-sized car. It will likely be priced similar to economy or midsize, but when they upgrade you, they do it based on the size, you rented and not the price (since you booked through a third party). The rental agent has a lot of sway here, so a good story and even a $20 tip might get you a huge upgrade.
	2. In Europe, they check your speed by radar and photo. They don’t even need to pull you over to give you a ticket (Ask Anne for details on this if you’re curious) Don’t speed (really!)! 
4. Local Transportation:
	1. If the cab has a meter, you’re probably ok. Don’t take a ride from the guys that offer at the exit of the airport. They tend to renegotiate once you arrive. Most major airports have fixed rates to different parts of the city. If you can take the train in from the airport to the city, you should – it’s faster and cheaper and safer most of the time.
	2. When hailing a cab, look for the little lights on the side of the top light to see if it’s available. Uber is not available in many countries and in many cities you have to go to a designated Uber pickup area at the airport. I don’t usually tip cab drivers, but if you get change, usually throw them a few bucks or say keep the change if it’s close. 
	3. Look for the last stop on the subway, in the direction you want to go. Look for that to know which way the train goes. The train and the metro are different. Usually, the metro is local, and almost always the kiosks to buy a train ticket and a metro ticket are different.
5. Hotels tips:
	1. If you like being pampered – get a fancy hotel! If you don’t care, try a flagship brand like Holiday Inn for consistency. If you’re adventurous, try Airbnb for short stays or VRBO for longer stays. I don’t like either for business travel because I need reliability. It can go either way for pleasurtravel – whatat’s important is your priority. In Russia we saved a bunch of money and got in the heart of the city using Airbnb. In [[On China|China]], we stayed in 5-star hotels for less than $200/night. Always choose location first — the closer you are to your activity/ restaurant scene/ etc the more like you are to get beyond the hotel lobby or restaurant next to the hotel. Every trip is different, but usually if a place has good customer reviews it’s probably going to be ok. 
	2. Vrbo is better for longer stays because people tend to rent out a vacation home or a rental property, verse having you sleep in their house. Remember, Airbnb and VRBO don’t always have things like coffee, more than one towel, shampoo, soap, or even an iron and ironing board. You can write a bad review, but there’s no front desk to call if there’s a problem, or you have a late check in due to a delayed flight. Big-city Airbnb is usually much cheaper than hotels and in that sense probably well worth the sacrifices. I wouldn’t book Airbnb more than a month in advance – this is a person, and they might change their mind and get out of the hotel business before your trip starts.
	3. Double bedrooms tend to be larger, even if there are only two of you. You also get double the pillows and towels!
6. Flights tips:
	1. You don’t need to remove your toiletries from your bag. 100’s of flights, and I’ve never taken mine out once.
	2. Print tickets in case your phone dies. If you have a tight connection on a small plane don’t carry on your bag. They will make you curb check it and it will take another 10-15 minutes. You’re better off checking it all the way through. On an international trip, never check your luggage if you don’t have to. Rule 7 — bring less. If they lose your luggage you’ll have way less! And spend your first few days shopping for basics like deodorant and underwear (on your vacation!). 
	3. Even with assigned seating, you want to get on early to have space in the overhead baggage compartment. If you have a tight layover, ask to sit Close to the front of the plane, so you can get off sooner. The alcohol is free on international flights, and you can ask for a double or even more if you'd like. You can (and should!) also always get more water in the rear Galley at anytime. Anne likes aisle seats because she usually drinks water constantly and uses the restroom frequently. Staying hydrated is important for battling jet lag and staying comfortable (dry eyes, headaches, etc.) If you ask for the whole can of pop they will usually give it to you.
	4. For international trips, the bulkhead row in coach gives the most legroom at the best price. If the plane is as three four three layout, the middle seat in the four is better than the middle seat of the 3 because no one has to climb over you to go to the bathroom. 
	5. You can also watch movies for free on your phone. For long trips, though, it’s a good idea to bring a book in case your phone dies, or the Wi-Fi goes out. 
	6. Drink lots of water on international flights. They lower the humidity to keep the plane from corroding, which is why you get so dry.
7. Flying attire:
	1. I love a long sleeve shirt with one or two front pockets. Front shirt pockets are great when you’re seated and belted. Long sleeves for when it gets cold. Always pants – if you sit by the window, it gets really cold by your legs. Don’t forget socks going through security and easy to remove shoes. Anne likes compression socks to keep feet and ankles from swelling. If you wear contacts, don’t wear them on the plane, your eyes will dry out. 
8. Shopping tips:
	1. If you’re going to shop for clothes, do it early in the trip before you get fat (or feel fat). No one likes the way they look on day 4 of a trip, and it’s not very fun to try on clothes.
	2. Don’t wait to get small stuff. You’ll forget and end up with airport trinkets.
	3. Wait to get the big stuff! However, always bring something fun and local home. $200 custom xyz bought locally is priceless – you can’t or wouldn’t buy it at home. It might be a little more than you wanted to spend, but it will be rewarding forever and much better than something you got at Target.
9. What’s in my carry on:
	- Travel pillow
	- headphones
	- eye mask
	- ear plugs
		- I flew for years with noise-canceling headphones until they broke and never looked back – happy for the added space and less worry.
	- Sewing kit
	- gum
	- umbrella
		- I like an umbrella because it covers my bag and backpack too.
	- Light pullover
		- The light pullover has been great in airports, cold meeting rooms, after a mustard spill, or when the weather changes.
	- Flashlight
	- deck of cards
	- USB charger
	- Power cable for USB charger power cable if different from your phone
	- Power cord & converters for phone
	- medical kit
	- granola/ power bar
	- pen
		- For ideas and customs cards. And cards are great for delays.
	- Sunglasses & Sunglasses case
	- change of underwear (optional)
		- Undies are listed as optional…until the day they aren’t. Having them is great if you end up on a sweaty flight with more travel to go, or your bags are lost.
	- Nail clippers
		- The nail clippers are a wonderful TSA approved tool for clipping, cutting, and picking.
	- Tooth brush and paste (optional)
		- After a long flight of mouth breathing, the tooth brush (or gum) is nice – gum is good for the ears too.
	- Book
	- For Work Trip:
		- My laptop and power cord.
