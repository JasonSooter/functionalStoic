# Strength Training

## Summary

Strength training, also known as resistance training or weight training, is a form of physical exercise designed to improve muscular fitness by exercising a specific muscle or muscle group against external resistance. Common forms of resistance include free weights, weight machines, or one's own body weight.

**Key Components of Strength Training:**

1. **Progressive Overload:** The fundamental principle that involves gradually increasing the amount of weight or resistance to challenge the muscles, promoting growth and increased strength.
2. **Repetitions and Sets:** Exercises are performed in a series of repetitions (reps) grouped into sets. A common structure might be three sets of ten repetitions.
3. **Recovery:** Adequate rest between workouts is crucial, as muscles need time to repair and grow. This often means working different muscle groups on alternating days.
4. **Variety of Exercises:** Incorporates a range of exercises targeting different muscle groups, such as squats, deadlifts, bench presses, and pull-ups, ensuring balanced muscle development.
5. **Form and Technique:** Proper form is critical to avoid injuries and ensure effective targeting of the muscles.
6. **Individualization:** Programs can be tailored to meet different goals, whether increasing strength, building muscle mass, enhancing endurance, or improving overall fitness.

**Benefits of Strength Training:**

- **Increased Muscle Mass:** Helps in building and maintaining muscle mass, which tends to decrease with age.
- **Enhanced Bone Density:** Weight-bearing exercises improve bone strength.
- **Improved Metabolic Rate:** Muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue.
- **Better Joint Function:** Strengthens muscles, tendons, and ligaments, providing better support to joints.
- **Enhanced Physical Performance:** Improves overall athletic performance by increasing strength and power.
- **Reduced Risk of Injury:** Strengthens muscles and connective tissues, making injuries less likely.

**Considerations:**

- **Safety:** It's crucial to learn proper techniques, possibly under the guidance of a trained professional, to prevent injuries.
- **Consistency:** Regular workouts are necessary for noticeable and lasting results.
- **Nutrition:** Adequate protein intake is important for muscle repair and growth.

Overall, strength training is a versatile form of exercise beneficial for people of all ages and fitness levels, contributing to overall health and well-being.

- Burpees
	- Tried-and-true go-to that exhausts your whole body
	- **Steps**
		- Start by standing with your feet shoulder-width apart and your arms at your sides. Bend your knees and lower down to a squat.
		- Put your hands on the floor, kick your legs back into a plank, and do a pushup. Then, jump your legs back in so that your feet land just outside of your hands, and stand up (or jump up). That’s one rep.
- Bear Crawls
	- This move looks a bit funny, but it’s worth it. The bear crawl activates multiple muscle groups and provides serious cardio.
	- **Steps**
		- Crouch down onto all fours, with your hands on the floor in front of you, your feet behind you, and your hips up in the air.
		- Your knees should be off the floor. Keeping your head up and eyes forward, crawl straight ahead. Once you’ve moved ten steps or come to a stopping point, perform the same crawling motion, but backward.
- Mountain Climbers
	- This classic cardio move also works your core, chest, and arms.
	- **Steps**
		- Start in the high plank position. Pull your right knee into your chest while keeping your abs tight. Quickly switch legs, returning your right leg to the starting position and moving your left knee into your chest.
		- Continue switching legs, simultaneously moving one knee in while the other goes back, similar to a running motion.
- Plank to Push-Up With Jacks
	- Multi-movement exercise that effectively targets your upper body, lower body, and core.
	- “The challenge is in your upper body, as you move from your elbows to your palms, and then you get in your legwork and an increase in cardio with your jacks.”
	- Steps
		- Start in a low plank on your elbows.
		- Raise up onto your palms, one arm at a time. Then jump your feet out to the sides and back in. Return to the starting position.
- Squats to Swim
	- This full-body move will have your arms, legs, and back burning—no weights required.
	- Steps
		- Start with your feet a little wider than shoulder-width and your arms straight out in front of you.
		- Squat down and, keeping your arms straight and at shoulder height, rotate them behind your back like you’re trying to touch your palms together.
		- Perform as one fluid motion rather than two separate movements. Once your legs are parallel to the floor, stand back up and return your arms to the starting position.
- Crab Walk Dips
	- Works your triceps, shoulders, core, and legs.
	- Steps
		- Start Position
			- Start on the floor in a dip position, the way you would while crawling backward.
			- Your stomach and face should face the ceiling
			- Your hands are slightly behind and outside your hips
			- Your knees bent
			- Feet flat on the floor
			- Your hips elevated a few inches off the ground.
		- Lower and raise your body to perform a tricep dip. Then, keeping that same raised position, crab walk forward on your hands and feet. Perform another dip, then crab walk back. Repeat.

## Figure Out Wolff's Law

- [ ] Kolt mentioned Wolff's Law. Figure out what it is
	- https://www.perplexity.ai/search/what-is-wolffs-law-ZTdcE66pT5qnu5xL8OvkzQ

Wolff's Law, developed by German anatomist Julius Wolff in the 19th century, explains how bones adapt to the mechanical stresses placed on them. When a bone experiences increased stress, it remodels itself to become stronger by altering its internal trabecular structure and external cortical layer. Conversely, reduced stress leads to weaker, less dense bones due to a lack of remodeling stimulus[1][3][5].

This process occurs through bone remodeling, where osteoclasts remove old or damaged bone tissue, and osteoblasts deposit new tissue. Mechanotransduction—a process converting mechanical signals into biochemical responses—drives these changes[3][5]. Wolff's Law is crucial in understanding bone health and is applied in physical therapy, osteoporosis prevention, and recovery from fractures through weight-bearing exercises[1]
