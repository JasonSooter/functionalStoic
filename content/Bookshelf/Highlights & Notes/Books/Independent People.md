## Role of the Bailiff and the Sheriff in *Independent People*

In *Independent People*, the bailiff and the sheriff symbolize institutional power structures that constrain individual freedom, especially for small farmers like Bjartur.

### Bailiff

- Represents local authority and the landowning class.
- Enforces economic obligations such as rent or debt collection.
- A liaison between the landowner (e.g., the wealthy merchants or nobles) and the tenant.
- Seen as the bureaucratic face of oppression—he applies the rules without personal animosity, but with complete indifference to human suffering.

### Sheriff

- Embodies the state's legal apparatus.
- Executes evictions, legal judgments, and court orders.
- Acts with formal legality, yet facilitates dispossession and systemic inequality.

### Thematic Role

- Together, they highlight Laxness’s critique of Iceland’s shift from communal peasant life to capitalist landholding systems.
- They reveal how "independence" in the novel is a loaded and often ironic ideal—used to justify suffering under economic coercion.
- Their presence reinforces the futility of Bjartur’s struggle against societal systems that are designed to ensure his failure.

### Literary Function

- They serve not only as characters but as symbols of mechanized and impersonal modernity encroaching on the pastoral myth.
- Their interventions escalate key turning points in the novel, especially regarding eviction and the erosion of family autonomy.

> In *Independent People*, Halldór Laxness uses the term "ling" to refer to a type of hardy, low-growing heath or shrub—most likely heather—that grows in the Icelandic moorlands and uplands. It is depicted as a common feature of the landscape, especially on islets in marshes and mountain slopes, and serves as both a natural element of the terrain and a practical resource, used for purposes such as fuel when mixed with moss. The presence of ling also indicates areas where sheep may graze or take shelter, making it significant to the pastoral life portrayed in the novel.
