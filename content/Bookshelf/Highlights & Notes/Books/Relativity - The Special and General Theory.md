> What did Einstein mean by practically rigid body in the first chapter of his book on relativity?

Amswer from Google Bard
- [source](https://g.co/bard/share/0e3e7adc89b7)
In the first chapter of his book "Relativity: The Special and General Theory," Einstein used the term "practically rigid body" in a specific way to build his theoretical framework. Here's how we can understand what he meant:

**1. Idealization:** A truly rigid body, meaning one that maintains its exact shape and size under any conditions, doesn't exist in the real world. It's a theoretical concept. Einstein acknowledged this, but for the purposes of developing his theory, he needed a reference point.

**2. Approximation:** To address this, he introduced the idea of a "practically rigid body." This refers to an object that, for the purposes of our observation and measurement, behaves very close to a true rigid body. In other words, its deformations due to forces or other factors are so small that we can practically ignore them for the specific situations we're considering.

**3. Importance in Special Relativity:** The concept of a practically rigid body plays a crucial role in establishing the fundamental principles of special relativity. Here's how:

- **Space and time measurements:** Einstein argued that if we have a system of coordinated measurements using rods and clocks attached to a "practically rigid body" at rest, these measurements would define a particular reference frame with fixed spatial and temporal properties.
- **Relative motion:** Observations made from different such reference frames in uniform motion relative to each other would still reveal the same laws of physics, according to the principle of relativity. This led to the famous concept of the constancy of the speed of light, regardless of the motion of the observer or source.

**4. Not perfect but useful:** It's important to remember that "practically rigid" is still an approximation. In more extreme situations or for extremely accurate measurements, even seemingly rigid objects would exhibit deformations. However, this concept provided a helpful starting point for developing the fundamental ideas of special relativity, which could then be applied to analyze real-world situations with appropriate adjustments.

**In summary, Einstein's use of "practically rigid body" helped him establish a reference frame for analyzing space, time, and motion in special relativity. While not perfectly realistic, it served as a useful tool for building the theoretical framework and understanding the basic principles of the theory.**
