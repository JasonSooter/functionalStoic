### 🚀 TL;DR (for Readwise highlight)

	 Eliot warns that even the noblest ambitions can be sunk by mundane, bodily realities—just as brave sailors get scurvy if they forget their lime-juice. The stanza foreshadows Dr Lydgate’s lofty medical ideals colliding with Middlemarch’s petty politics in Chapter 18.
	 ---

## Why This Epigraph?

	 | Image | Meaning |
	 |-------|---------|
	 | **“Loftiest hopes” draw lots with “meaner hopes”** | Grand ideals must risk the same random outcomes as everyday ambitions. |
	 | **“Bad air” & pestilence** | Heroes breathe the same miasmas as everyone else; physical reality levels social hierarchy. |
	 | **Crossing the Line without lime-juice** | Sailors who ignore practical details (vitamin C) succumb to scurvy; lofty ventures hinge on humble precautions. |
	 The stanza insists that moral heroism is worthless without attention to material constraints.

### Source Status

	 Scholars classify the lines as **Eliot’s own invention**—a mock-classical epigraph with no earlier literary source.

### How it Primes Chapter 18

	 - **Plot cue:** Chapter 18 shows Dr Lydgate’s first major setback—the town’s fever-hospital chaplain election. 
	 - **Moral lesson:** Like a sailor forgetting his limes, Lydgate neglects the “nutrients” of success (alliances, custom, funding) while chasing high medical ideals. 
	 - **Thematic echo:** Bodily illness (typhus) mirrors civic “pestilence” in Middlemarch’s politics, underscoring the link between biology and ethics that the epigraph dramatizes.

### Extra Insight

	 By composing her own epigraph, Eliot stages the tension she describes: a “lofty” poetic flourish immediately meets the prosaic social reality that follows, reminding us reform demands biochemical humility as much as moral zeal.

- Alban Mountains ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k5kmht288ajtn19q9dpe33fh))
> The “Alban Mountains” in your quotation are not in Britain or the Balkans at all but in Italy—what English‐speakers usually call the Alban Hills (Italian: Colli Albani or Monti Albani). Here’s what you need to know:
   1. Location 
   • About 15–20 km southeast of central Rome, in the region of Lazio. 
   • They form a roughly circular cluster of hills rising out of the Roman Campagna. 
   2. Geology & Topography 
   • Volcanic origin—an extinct volcanic complex whose last activity was some 36,000 years ago. 
   • Highest peak is Monte Cavo (ca. 950 m above sea level). 
   • At the heart lie two small crater lakes, Lake Albano and Lake Nemi. 
   3. Historical & Cultural Significance 
   • The area around Lake Albano was sacred to early Romans and to the Latin League; according to legend Alba Longa, mother city of Rome, stood here. 
   • In post-classical times it became rural retreats for Roman nobles and, later, popes (Castel Gandolfo, the papal summer residence, is on its western flank). 
   • Even today the vistas from the Albans—toward the Apennines, the Campagna, or across to Rome—have been prized by painters, poets and travelers (much as the Pincian Hill in Rome itself). 
   4. Modern Day 
   • Protected in large part by the Regional Park of the Castelli Romani. 
   • Famous for vineyards (the Frascati wine district lies on its lower slopes), olive groves and chestnut woods. 
   In short, when the writer speaks of seeing something “as noble as the Alban Mountains,” he’s invoking the rolling, wooded volcanic heights just southeast of Rome—long a byword for classic Italian landscape and grand views over the Roman plain.
