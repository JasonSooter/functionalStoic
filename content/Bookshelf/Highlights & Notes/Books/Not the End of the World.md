## Summary

**Feeling anxious, powerless or confused about the future of our planet? This** **book will transform how you see our biggest environmental problems - and how we can solve them

We are bombarded by doomsday headlines that tell us the soil won't be able to support crops, fish will vanish from our oceans, that we should reconsider having children.

But in this bold, radically hopeful book, data scientist Hannah Ritchie argues that if we zoom out, a very different picture emerges. The data shows we've made so much progress on these problems, and so fast, that we could be on track to achieve true sustainability for the first time in history.

Packed with the latest research, practical guidance and enlightening graphics, this book will make you rethink almost everything you've been told about the environment, from the virtues of eating locally and living in the countryside, to the evils of overpopulation, plastic straws and palm oil. It will give you the tools to understand what works, what doesn't and what we urgently need to focus on so we can leave a sustainable planet for future generations.

These problems are big. But they _are_ solvable. We are not doomed. We can build a better future for everyone. Let's turn that opportunity into reality.

A good fit for people who are looking for a hopeful and informative book that focuses on solving environmental problems and building a sustainable future, without being overly doomsday or preachy.

Unlike the arithmetic mean, the geometric mean is more appropriate for data that spans several orders of magnitude or is multiplicative in nature.

As the Head of Research at this organization, Ritchie emphasizes its role in making complex data accessible and understandable to the general public, thereby fostering informed discussions about pressing societal issues. The platform serves as a vital resource for researchers, policymakers, and the public, providing updated insights and fostering a better understanding of the trajectories of various global problems.

> Q: Did [[Hans Rosling]] have any connection to Our World in Data? And if so, what was the connection?

> A: [[Hans Rosling]] did not directly found Our World in Data, but his work and philosophy significantly influenced its mission and approach. Max Roser, the founder of Our World in Data, has often referenced Rosling's emphasis on data-driven insights and public understanding of global trends, reflecting the shared commitment to improving global literacy through data visualization and research.
> q: Did [[Hans Rosling]] start both Gapminder and Our World in Data?
> a: [[Hans Rosling]] co-founded Gapminder, but he did not start Our World in Data. Our World in Data was initiated by Max Roser and his team, focusing on providing data and research on global living conditions.
> q: How is Gapminder connected to Our World in Data?
> a: Gapminder and Our World in Data share a common goal of promoting data-driven understanding of global development issues. Both platforms utilize data visualization to present information on various social, economic, and environmental indicators, making complex data accessible to a wider audience. Additionally, they often complement each other by providing users with different perspectives and tools for exploring global trends.
> Gapminder: A non-profit organization founded in 2005 by Swedish statistician [[Hans Rosling]], along with his son, Ola Rosling, and daughter-in-law, Anna Rosling Rönnlund. Gapminder aims to promote a fact-based worldview by providing accessible data visualization tools that illustrate global development trends. It is best known for its interactive software, such as the Gapminder World, which allows users to explore various indicators of human development, including health, income, and education, over time and across countries. The organization emphasizes the importance of statistical literacy and critical thinking in understanding global issues, often challenging misconceptions about poverty, development, and environmental sustainability.
