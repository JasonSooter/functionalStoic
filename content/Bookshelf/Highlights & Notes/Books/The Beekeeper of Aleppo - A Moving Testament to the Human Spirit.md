> This Syriac proverb demonstrates the impossibility of absolutely knowing the personality and soul of the other person, even despite the length and strength of the relationship.
   Yes, that's a powerful and significant quote from Christy Lefteri's "The Beekeeper of Aleppo." Here's what I've found:
   * The quote's essence:
   * It highlights the idea that even in our closest relationships, there are hidden depths and complexities within individuals that remain unknown.
   * It speaks to the inherent mystery of human nature and the fact that people are constantly evolving and experiencing things that shape them in unseen ways.
   * It is also noted that the quote is described as a Syrian saying.
   * Context in the book:
   * In "The Beekeeper of Aleppo," this quote gains particular resonance due to the characters' experiences of trauma and displacement. War and hardship force them to confront aspects of themselves that they may not have previously known.
   * The quote is used in the context of Nuri's reflections on his wife Afra. The trauma they experienced changed them both, and caused a distance between them.
   * Significance:
   * The quote encourages empathy and understanding, reminding us that everyone carries their own burdens and hidden stories.
   * It also speaks to the profound impact of trauma and how it can reshape a person's identity.
   In essence, the quote serves as a poignant reminder of the multifaceted nature of human beings.
