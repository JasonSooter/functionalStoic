- Squire Make-a-stir ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k668taby6egzaj5t94ejqk9t))
> Squire Make-a-stir (no other given name; sometimes simply called the Weston squire) is sketched only briefly by Thoreau as a typical country squire—respectable in dress and bearing though not physically detailed. He serves as a minor but emblematic local landowner and patron of servants and hounds, around whom market gossip and hunting activity revolve; proud, fame‑seeking, meddlesome, and solicitous of deference. Little personal history is provided beyond his appearances in hunt‑and‑market anecdotes, and his principal ties are to the teamster (his driver/servant) and the Concord hunter (neighbor and interlocutor about the hounds).
- Wilberforce ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k6691w7wm8ktmvvkkzmfx3hm))
> Wilberforce (William Wilberforce), invoked in Walden, appears not as a dramatized figure but as the historical British abolitionist whose name Thoreau summons as a symbol; no physical description is offered. His role in the text is emblematic — a moral emancipator set against the timid, fame‑seeking men who "cower and sneak," representing resolute reform and conscience. Backstory: the real Wilberforce led the movement to abolish the slave trade in Britain; key ties in Thoreau's usage include Thoreau himself (invoker/critic), the "cowering" conformist (contrast), and "public opinion" (the social force his example confronts).
