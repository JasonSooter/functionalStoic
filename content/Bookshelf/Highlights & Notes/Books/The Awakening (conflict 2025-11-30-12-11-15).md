- rather handsome than beautiful. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01karz5x665cnj26cmfynpv87f))
> Mrs. Edna Pontellier (often called Edna), sometimes described in narrative as "rather handsome than beautiful," is a striking woman of about thirty, with heavy yellow‑brown hair, yellowish‑brown eyes, and a frank, candid face—handsome rather than classically beautiful. She is the novel’s protagonist whose awakening toward artistic independence and personal desire drives the plot, fracturing her conventional roles as wife and mother. Independent, introspective, and increasingly defiant, Edna rebels against social expectations (Léonce Pontellier — husband, possessive and conventional), forms intense emotional attachments that unsettle her marriage (Robert Lebrun — close friend and object of longing), and maintains complex friendships that contrast models of motherhood and conformity (Madame Ratignolle — confidante and exemplar of maternal devotion; Mademoiselle Reisz — critical artistic mentor).
- Chénière ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01karzc6fz722b6w87rhm6n7en))
> Chénière Caminada (often shortened to the Chénière) — the quiet, grassy streeted islet of the Chénière Caminada off Grand Isle — is a sandy, windswept spit of land with water oaks, white beaches, and a small cluster of cottages and a chapel reached by boat. It serves as a seasonal, intimate retreat in The Awakening where characters go to mass, promenade, play, and enact private encounters that reveal social habits and the protagonists’ growing restlessness and desires. Associated persons and events include Edna Pontellier’s awakenings and liaisons (sites of emotional freedom and drifting away from domestic anchors), Robert Lebrun’s flirtations with Edna (shared outings and boat crossings to the Chénière), and local social gatherings such as croquet under the oaks and the chapel mass (community rituals and whispered romances).

> Old Monsieur Farival (sometimes called Monsieur Farival) is an elderly, bronzed-faced Creole gentleman with white silky hair and mustache, often seen in a big straw hat and bearing the deliberate military carriage of a retired colonel. He appears as a persistent, sociable fixture at Grand Isle—grandfather of the Farival twins and a frequent participant in the Lebrun household’s gatherings—offering advice, telling stories, fussing over interruptions, and contributing comic nagging and nautical know-how. Affable, pedantic, and occasionally querulous, he is a proud former Confederate officer whose past is hinted at in plantation reminiscences; his closest ties are to the Farival twins (grandfather/guardian), Madame Lebrun and her household (longtime family friends and fellow summer guests), and the social circle of Grand Isle including Robert and Edna Pontellier (acquaintances and conversational companions).

Brusque, petty at times (bickering with Monsieur Farival, grumbling about Mariequita), and competent at his trade, he is tied chiefly to Robert Lebrun (boatmate and fellow voyager) and to the seasonal summer visitors including Edna Pontellier (passenger whose moods his craft helps to mirror).
