# Aristotle the Scientist

changelog/2021/10/08

page 4

> Scientists do not advance either as a result of mere accumulation of knowledge, or by means of absolute revolutions in which everything is thrown out, and we begin again from zero. They advance instead as in a wonderful [[Analogy]] first made by #people/authors/otto-neurath and frequently cited by Quine #people/authors/willard-van-orman-quine, like sailors who must rebuild their ship, on the open sea never able to start fresh from the bottom, where a beam is taken away and new one must at once be put there and for this the rest of the ship is used as support. In this way, the ship can be shaped entirely a new but only by gradual reconstruction.
> In the great ship of modern physics, we can still recognize its ancient structures as first laid out in the old ship of Aristotelian thought.

I like this [[Analogy]], it ties in with #concepts/marginal-gains-theory because it implies the need to make continual improvements without actually being able to start from scratch.

## Newton the Alchemist

#🗓️/2021/10/21

time::12 minutes

Discussion of Newton's interest in Alchemy. The conclusion of Rovelli is that this is a good example that's shows both the limits of Newton while also using his lack of publishing anything related to Alchemy as reinforcement of "the basis of the science that he helped to give birth to", i.e. Modern Science and the Scientific [[Methods|Method]]

> I think that the genius of Newton lay precisely in being aware of these limits: the limits of what he did not know. And that is the basis of the science that he helped to give birth to
