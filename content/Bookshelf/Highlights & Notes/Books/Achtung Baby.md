"Achtung Baby" by Sara Zaske explores the author's experiences and observations while raising her family in [[Germany]]. The book highlights the cultural differences in parenting between [[Germany]] and the United States.

## Summary

Zaske contrasts the German emphasis on [[Independence]], [[Independence|responsibility]], and risk-taking with the more cautious and controlled approach often seen in American parenting. She delves into various aspects, including the education system, outdoor activities, and the concept of "selbständigkeit" ([[Independence|self-reliance]]), shedding light on how these cultural factors shape parenting practices.

## Key Takeaways

1. **[[Independence]] and [[Independence|Responsibility]]**
	- German parents encourage their children to be more independent and take [[Independence|responsibility]] from a young age. This fosters [[Independence]] and [[Resillience]]
2. **Risk-Taking:**
	- Germans are more accepting of allowing children to engage in activities with an element of risk, promoting a sense of adventure and confidence.
3. **Balanced Education:** The German education system is discussed, emphasizing a more holistic approach that values creativity, practical skills, and a balance between academics and other activities.
4. **Outdoor Play:** The importance of outdoor play and exposure to nature is highlighted in German parenting, contributing to physical [[health]] and well-being.
5. **Parental Involvement:** While [[Independence]] is encouraged, there is still a strong emphasis on parental involvement, creating a supportive and nurturing environment.
6. **Cultural Perspective:** The book encourages readers to reflect on their own cultural perspectives and consider adopting elements from different parenting styles to create a more balanced and effective approach.

In essence, "Achtung Baby" provides insights into the German parenting model, prompting readers to reevaluate their own parenting practices and consider a more open-minded and balanced approach that prioritizes [[Independence]], [[Independence|responsibility]], and a sense of adventure in children.

***On the relationship I want with my children***

Perhaps the hardest thing to do is to give our growing children intellec tual and spiritual freedom. We all want to raise children to become adults who hold the same values as we do, but no matter what we do, there is no guarantee that they will. Imposing our own belief systems on our children is likely to backfire, causing them to rebel, or may permanently damage our relationships with them as adults.

Do you think exactly like your parents do? On every topic? There's a good reason it's a cultural cliché to never discuss politics or religion at Thanksgiving dinner. Some of the most diverse and conflicting ideas are found among members of immediate families. So why then do we hang on to the practice of trying to mold our children into smaller versions of ourselves?

As parents, we can exercise an enormous amount of power over our children if we want to. We have physical, legal, and financial control over their lives for quite some time. If we misuse that power to try to force them to think the way we do, or do exactly what we want, our children may not forgive us when they get older.

"I'm looking at the long-term picture," the German father Axel told me. "I'm looking at me at eighty. How do I want my kids to be with me I'm not talking about them taking care of me when I'm fragile… but the emotional connection to them."

With two teenage children just a few years from adulthood, Axel real izes that they will soon have their own lives, and other people and activities will become more important to them than their parents. Still, he would want them to know that he is always there for them, without judgment. "I would love for them to have that connection that if they have problems or find themselves in a really bad situation, they know they can come back and not have me say, 'Oh, I told you so," he said.

I like Axel's vision of the parent-child relationship, which builds respect and trust to last a lifetime. I hope I can do this with my children as well. I no longer expect to raise children who will agree with me.
