- The Bulgarians and the Abares ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kcxvwf5nn7psdeadefkeeakd))
> In Candide, the Bulgarians and the Abares aren’t meant to be taken as the real historical peoples you’d find on a map. They’re Voltaire’s stand-ins for contemporary European armies, used to satirize war (especially the Seven Years’ War).
   Bulgarians ≈ Prussians (Frederick the Great’s army) 
   Abares (Abars/Avars) ≈ the French (their wartime opponent in the allegory) 
   Why he does it:
   Censorship / deniability + universality: swapping in “exotic” names lets him attack real governments while pretending it’s “about someone else.” 
   Moral symmetry of war: in the Bulgar–Abar war chapters, Voltaire shows both sides doing the same “legal” barbarism—burned villages, raped civilians, bodies everywhere—then singing Te Deum like it’s holy work. 
   One extra sharp edge: some editors/critics note Voltaire likely enjoyed the “Bulgar” label partly because it echoes French bougre (“bugger/scoundrel”), a jab aimed at Prussia/Frederick in particular.
