## Summary

The moving, playful memoir of [[Hans Rosling]], Swedish statistics mastermind, researcher extraordinaire and author of the global bestseller, Factfulness.

This is a book that contains very few numbers. Instead, it is about meeting people who have opened my eyes. It was facts that helped him explain how the world works. But it was [[curiosity]] and commitment that made the late [[Hans Rosling]], author of worldwide bestseller Factfulness, the most popular researcher of our time.

How I Learned to Understand the World is his own story of how a young scientist learned to become a revolutionary thinker, and takes us from the swelter of an emergency clinic in [[#Mozambique]] to the World Economic Forum at Davos. In collaboration with Swedish journalist Fanny Härgestam, Hans wrote his memoir with the same joy of storytelling that made a whole world listen when he spoke.

*"How I Learned to Understand the World"* is a memoir by [[Hans Rosling]], a notable Swedish physician, academic, and public speaker known for his work in global health and statistics. In this book, Rosling shares personal stories and insights from his life and career, illustrating how his experiences shaped his understanding of the world.

The memoir begins with Rosling’s early life in [[Sweden]], highlighting his upbringing and the influence of his family. He discusses his education and how his [[curiosity]] and desire to address global issues led him to study medicine and public health. Throughout his career, Rosling worked in various countries, often in challenging conditions, which provided him with firsthand insights into global health disparities and development issues.

A significant portion of the book covers his work in [[#Mozambique]] and other developing countries, where he conducted research on diseases such as konzo and worked on health initiatives. These experiences helped him recognize the importance of data and evidence-based decision-making in improving public health outcomes.

Rosling also explains his passion for teaching and spreading knowledge, which ultimately led him to become an influential speaker who used innovative methods to make complex data understandable and engaging. His TED Talks and the founding of Gapminder Foundation, which developed tools to present global statistics visually, are key parts of his legacy.

Throughout the memoir, Rosling emphasizes the importance of questioning assumptions, understanding complex systems, and using data to counter myths about the world. He advocates for a fact-based worldview and encourages readers to seek out and understand the complexities behind pressing global issues.

The book is not only an account of Rosling's life but also a call to action for individuals to adopt a more informed and empathetic perspective on global challenges.

- Hans, Agneta, and the children would roam around the European continent in their white Volvo, packed to the rafters and with a roof box on top. Hans always brought at least one suitcase full of unread academic literature, but often got sucked into a doorstop-size novel. They camped and changed site daily, sleeping in a green tent bought from a classified ad. It ranked as “Eastern Europe’s ugliest,” said Agneta, “the kind of tent everyone had stopped using.” On skis Hans collected countries. When all the bigger ones had been ticked off the list, they went for the smaller and the more distant territories—Andorra, Monaco, and so on. They were not allowed to put a tick against a country until they had eaten something there. They carried a gas-fired [[camping]] stove but meals were never great performances in the Rosling family. Eating amounted to fuel provision, to keep you going, and bread was as good as anything. Agneta fixed herself cups of instant coffee in the boot of the car—except for the year when Hans forgot the stove, but managed to pack a portable fax machine. “His mind prioritized his paperwork above our family packing,” Agneta commented.
