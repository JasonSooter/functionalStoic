## Highlights

- When I arrived in Denmark more than a decade ago, I was struck by how *non-competitive* the sporting culture was. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vbtnhj31rv9tdnw582g3h))

> This would be a refreshing characteristic

- Here was a nation that cycled not for speed, but because it was practical. They ran not to punish themselves, but because it made them feel good. There was no shame in being a beginner, no pressure to win. Just a shared appreciation of *fællesskab* – community – and the joy of moving your body. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vdcecd2ty35xks9r4neqh))
- A Danish friend once told me that the national attitude to movement can be summed up as: “You don’t have to be the best, you just have to *join in.”* ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vfcgpgjqx7fya4gk0zp97))
- According to the 2022 Active Healthy Kids Global Alliance report, Denmark and Finland top the polls when it comes to keeping children active - 82 per cent of Danish children ‘actively transport themselves’ to school twice a week, with 60 per cent doing so daily. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vg38m752y4qcssdg83a1w))
- Sport is embedded into everyday life for adults, too, with many cycling to work (there are famously more bikes than cars in Copenhagen) or joining clubs or associations to keep active outside office hours. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vh3kp9wzsm8z5xk0qbrq9))
- Research backs this up: people who feel more connected to their communities report higher levels of wellbeing. And physical activity, especially with others, has been shown to significantly reduce symptoms of depression and anxiety. It’s a two-for-one deal that’s available to almost everyone. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jw3vm1j7hqq6ff2pbfae74f0))
