- There are three types of friendship, Aristotle says. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtsh0dmyq7nj1kb3a48j2q))
- The first one is based on pleasure ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtsz6kqm8gsda1paaepdky))
- the way our relationship in high school was all about laughing, or the way you can date someone because they are hot and make you feel good. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtta5m6dpksbmzbp6hbmw0))
- The second is based on utility ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdttr7sh6nmqwz4tcn44d74))
- you’re friends because it is useful, because your friend plays drums and you need a drummer in your band, or you’re friends because the other person is high status and gives you access to resources (though, that is a little embarrassing to admit so you lie to yourself and say that the reason you like them has nothing to do with status hunger). ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtv5kjx7evbxmammb4999g))
- The third and deepest kinds of friendship, according to Aristotle, are *friendships of virtue*. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtwcgafkn80gnqy6mbga75))
- That is, you like a person because of their character. They might be fun, they might be useful, but even if they aren’t, you like them anyway. It is what the marriage vows are getting at with the line: “To have and to hold, from this day forward, for better, for worse, for richer, for poorer, in sickness and in health, to love and to cherish, till death do us part.” Is this the kind of person that, if they were old and ugly and had soiled their pants and needed your help, then you’d still say, Yes? “Yes, I would love to. I want to give this person my care so they can unfold their life with dignity.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jtdtx4hrsgch75be53vs5s3y))
