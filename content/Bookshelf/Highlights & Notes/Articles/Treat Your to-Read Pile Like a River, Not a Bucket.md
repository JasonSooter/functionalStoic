- Category: #articles
- Summary: The text discusses the problem of having too much to read or do, leading to overwhelm and stress. Rather than trying to fit everything in, the author suggests embracing the idea that it's impossible to do it all and making tough choices about what truly matters. By treating your to-read pile like a river, you can pick and choose what to focus on, freeing yourself from feeling obligated to tackle everything.

## Highlights

- My challenge, information-wise, isn't about finding a needle in a haystack. It's that I'm confronted on a daily basis, in Carr's words, by "haystack-sized piles of needles." ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01hzzksq0xwf7ahcr4dx9wk5m2))
- the only way to deal with a too-many-needles problem is to confront the fact that it's insoluble – that you definitely won't be fitting everything in. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01hzzkwrf9emwpc8wtkjhnn12k))
- It's not a question of rearranging your to-do list so as to [make space for all your "big rocks"](https://www.oliverburkeman.com/so/60NWXZixI/c?w=g9EKf5Lv4dlKvqPm06dCQrm_h5m7KtU3D_wcpDzjNds.eyJ1IjoiaHR0cDovL3d3dy5hcHBsZXNlZWRzLm9yZy9iaWctcm9ja3NfY292ZXkuaHRtIiwiciI6ImEwOGRhOGFkLTlkMTktNGUyYS02OTdhLTE4ODMwZWNhM2JkOCIsIm0iOiJscCJ9), but of accepting that there are simply too many rocks to fit in the jar. You have to take a stab at deciding what matters most, among your various creative passions/life goals/responsibilities – and then do that, while acknowledging that you'll inevitably be neglecting many other things that matter too. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01hzzkxbecq50nw8b2jw5arjfw))
- To return to information overload: this means treating your "to read" pile like a river (a stream that flows past you, and from which you pluck a few choice items, here and there) instead of a bucket (which demands that you empty it). After all, you presumably don't feel overwhelmed by all the unread books in the British Library – and not because there aren't [an overwhelming number of them](https://www.oliverburkeman.com/so/60NWXZixI/c?w=qiSKQRmJJq0-qcwQTuOai0NtmSDVPnNWqEd4D_sHVwI.eyJ1IjoiaHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuYmwudWsvYWJvdXQtdXMvb3VyLXN0b3J5L2ZhY3RzLWFuZC1maWd1cmVzLW9mLXRoZS1icml0aXNoLWxpYnJhcnkjIiwiciI6ImEwOGRhOGFkLTlkMTktNGUyYS02OTdhLTE4ODMwZWNhM2JkOCIsIm0iOiJscCJ9), but because it never occurred to you that it might be your job to get through them all ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01hzzkywcd4ekzhmrqn78bz4dj))
- Coming at life this way definitely entails tough choices. But it's liberating, too, as you slowly begin to grasp that you never had any other option. There's no point beating yourself up for failing to clear a backlog (of unread books, undone tasks, unrealized dreams) that it was always inherently unfeasible to clear in the first place. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01hzzkzv3cs88e0d803491ea3e))
