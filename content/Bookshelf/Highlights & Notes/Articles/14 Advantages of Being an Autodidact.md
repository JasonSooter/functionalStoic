### The Top 14 Advantages of Being an Autodidact
#### **1**. **You can pursue your curiosities at will.**
- “The class is moving on, so you must too!” That’s something you’ll never have to hear as an autodidact. No one can steal your chance to heed the callings of your soul. No authority can prevent you from departing from a curriculum to explore an authentic interest. As an autodidact, you are free to follow that shy inner voice, wherever it may lead. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42mbqhyh24gpvnchn61ggk))
#### 2. You form an original point of view.
- By forming your personal curriculum and choosing your own books, articles, and online courses, you develop an original mind that leads to an original perspective—or, [philosophy of life](https://samuelrinko.substack.com/p/broad-self-study-the-key-to-creative)—which will help you approach problems and topics in a unique way. Want an extra boon to your originality? [Consider reading old books](https://knowledgelust.com/6-practical-reasons-to-read-old-books-consult-the-writers-of-the-past/) as well. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42nsmmkhn04sdqfyne5tsh))
#### 3. You can move on if something’s not worth your time.
- If a concept or technique is not helpful to your end-goal, or just plain uninteresting, then you can skip over it, rather than having to study it for the upcoming quiz, regardless of its futility and drudgery. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42qmx7ceqn6q1c1zzzbmqk))
#### 4. You can learn at your ideal pace.
- You’ll never fall behind or be held back by the slower students. Concepts that come easily to you can be dealt with in days. And you never have to rush through your readings and miss valuable information. If an idea or book interests you, you can ponder it for weeks before jumping into the next one. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42rde3v3f88921hqn1x44h))
#### 5. You get to use learning materials that work for you.
- Hate lectures because your mind wanders? Use essays and books instead. Favor hands-on learning? Start a project. As an autodidact, you can develop a self-education plan that works for your particular learning style, as opposed to what the administrator prescribes. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42sxrs6bdqhj3tetxtw74v))
#### 6. You avoid learning methods that clash with your preferences.
- Despise group projects? Do a solo one. Can’t stand multiple choice quizzes but like to write? Use essays as your form of retrieval practice. This keeps you from using methods that you hate. And, as a rule, the best study methods are the ones you can stick with for the long term—provided of course they are sound methods ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42vqvh5qhxxdaq7pap6br2))
#### 7. You save thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars.
- The [costs of degree programs](https://educationdata.org/average-cost-of-college) keep going up. Even one-time courses taught at colleges can cost a month’s wages. When you [craft a self-education plan](https://samuelrinko.substack.com/p/how-to-create-a-12-week-self-education?utm_source=activity_item), you can make it affordable. In fact, a lot of learning material is free — online courses, blogs, YouTube videos. And the stuff you do buy (like books!), you can make sure fits within a reasonable budget. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42x9kdrmganhjfg091mx3f))
#### 8. You can experiment as much as you want.
- With no self-consciousness that a teacher or classmate is going to laugh at your work, you can try new stuff and suck in obscurity, provided of course that you have a well-trained trash detector. That’s easier in skateboarding than in writing. But still, for the shy type, this is good in the beginning, as long as you eventually go on to get feedback. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka42zdhcj3wm7ebx3dhbgcha))
#### 9. You can learn anything you want.
- When you’re a self-learner, there are no restrictions on what you can learn. You don’t have to skip that Intro to Russian Literature course you really want to take because you have to get credits for your degree requirements. The world of knowledge is yours to explore. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka430mpjwep443xxmxs0mtjb))
#### 10. You learn self-motivation and self-discipline.
- In self-directed learning, there’s usually no one holding you accountable but yourself. A skilled autodidact is one who can stay the course without losing steam, who can do their reading or practice even when they don’t feel like it. Self-learning, therefore, forces you to improve your powers of self-motivation and discipline, which apply to all areas of work and life. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka431mrmb31w4y5ssga7fa7x))
#### 11. Learning never feels like a chore.
- In any formal program, you run the risk of the thing you’re excited to learn starting to feel like work. In elementary school I loved to read novels. In 8th grade I swore off books forever. I had associated them with homework, the thief of my play time. Fortunately, I found my way back. But I missed so much time I could’ve been reading! Similarly, [Benjamin Mcevoy](https://www.youtube.com/@BenjaminMcEvoy), one of my favorite online literature teachers, said he didn’t read for 2 years after graduating from Oxford’s English program. So the benefit: autodidactism ensures learning feels like play, so you do it more often and with the spirit of adventure. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka434943b0jf1cjcckf9j74k))
#### 12. You become great at planning.
- Scott Young, famous for learning the MIT computer science curriculum on his own, recommends in his book [Ultralearning](https://amzn.to/3U4bt4i) that you spend at least 10% of the total study time planning out your learning strategy. Doing this planning many times – organizing materials, setting goals and deadlines, structuring my learning in a logical and systematic way — has taken me from D- planner to a B+, a skill that’s helped me with business, marketing, and other aspects of my life. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka4352jn46banpqfh5g6t4br))
#### 13. You learn how to teach yourself.
- Teaching yourself is a skill. One that will repay you a thousand times over for the time you took to learn it. Like any skill, it requires practice, and that’s exactly what you do whenever you learn something autodidact-mode. You learn what learning methods work for you, how to plan a course of study, how to identify your learning needs and challenges, and more. If you ever do go into a formal program, you’ll be better equipped to succeed, because as Parker Settecase said in the [Autodidact Spotlight interview](https://samuelrinko.substack.com/p/autodidact-spotlight-2-parker-settecase), all learning is self-directed, whether it’s in school or not. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka436t4g3dgy43pwgr7fqjj8))
#### 14. You gain self-confidence
- It’s empowering to learn a new subject or skill on your own, whether it’s something academic like [the basics of political philosophy](https://samuelrinko.substack.com/p/4-books-to-self-study-political-philosophy) or something skill-based like [content writing](https://knowledgelust.com/how-to-self-learn-content-writing-8-steps-from-a-freelance-blogger/). You prove to yourself that you are capable of learning. You alter your identity from someone who needs to be taught to someone who can teach themself, and then you have the confidence to go and learn something even more challenging, until next thing you know you’re an expert. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01ka438xmx6b18310a2r427vqg))
