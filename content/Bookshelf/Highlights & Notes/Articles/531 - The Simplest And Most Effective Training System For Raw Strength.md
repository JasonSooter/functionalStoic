## The 5/3/1 Philosophy

### Emphasize Big, Multi-Joint Movements

- This really isn’t any secret. Beginners have been told to do this for years, and advanced lifters swear by these movements. Multi-joint lifts are lifts that involve more than one muscle – i.e., not an isolation exercise like leg extensions – and allow you to build the most muscle. These lifts are the most efficient for building muscle and strength. Examples are the squat, deadlift, bench press and power clean. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvprr1395tr0mbjv7envypyb))

### Start Too Light

- My coaches emphasized this to me when I was in high school, but unfortunately, I didn’t listen. Hopefully you will. Starting too light allows for more time for you to progress forward. It’s easy for anyone – beginner or advanced – to want to get ahead of themselves. Your lifts will go up for a few months, but then they’ll stall – and stall, and stall some more. Lifters get frustrated and don’t understand that the way around this is to prolong the time it takes to get to the goal. You have to keep inching forward. This is a very hard pill to swallow for most lifters. They want to start heavy, and they want to start now. This is nothing more than ego, and nothing will destroy a lifter faster, or for longer, than ego. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvprrrks2ky48x0193drx3r2))
- This goes hand in hand with starting light. Slow progress might not get you the best rewards today, but it will tomorrow. The longer you can progress, even if it’s by one rep or 2.5 pounds,
	the more it means that you’re actually *making* progress. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvprsa08frgyq5keccwhzs3r))

### The Squat

- Your eyes should be focused. Some people believe you should look straight up when you squat. These people aren’t good to listen to. Your eyes should be directed straight ahead or just slightly downward. Don’t take your eyes off the point you choose. Pick something, and stare at it intently. Even if there’s movement and distractions around this point, they shouldn’t stop your stare. This is how focused you have to be. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyp54e1zhpp8vrqnzg8prb))
- Proper bar placement depends on your body type and what’s most comfortable to you. Some people have shoulder problems and can’t carry the bar very low. Others just plain suck at high bar squatting. Place the bar where it allows you to reach depth with good form. It’s just that easy. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjypsj627x57tqgb6074q6n))
- I like taking a full grip on the bar, but I used a “thumbs-around” grip for the first half of my squatting life. I don’t see this as a deal-breaker. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyrn4e7gamdkfqrhge7cpx))
- Keep your elbows down and try to force them under the bar. This will cause your hips to drive first out of the bottom of the squat. It’ll also keep your chest high and prevent you from squatting with your legs first instead of your back. The first thing to shoot up with many people is their ass. Remedy this by pushing with your hips and keeping your elbows under the bar – or at least trying to. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyrvakrg7rgp5pr7ckchrj))
- Descend until the tops of your thighs are parallel to the ground. Go deeper if you’d like, but this is the minimum depth you should shoot for. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyteay5ne4j05yymyytkb0))
- When you begin your descent, push your knees out to the sides and your glutes back. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyv0k1vcj3nxvmqr2afkv6))
- Arch your upper back hard for the entire lift. This will cause your lower back to arch, too. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyvattybfdm1a4y00n1ht3))
- Grip the bar with the narrowest grip you can manage without hurting your shoulders. This will ensure that you remain tight throughout the lift. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyyagpz8zdma4sbzz23zys))
- Once you hit parallel, drive your elbows under the bar and explode up. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyyk2hbkk2esktceb7zg3s))
- • Before you take the bar out of the rack, fill your diaphragm with air, place the bar on your back, then confidently push it out of the rack with your back and legs. Don’t “wimp” the bar out. I like to do this with a large breath, which I won’t let out until I’m in my stance. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjyz856ve7jcpenfez1pjh8))
- Any more than two or three steps back is a waste of time and energy. Be efficient. I mentally count, “One, Two” when taking the bar out to make sure that I take only two steps out of the rack. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjz0yw1cr9pwv7v9ycf9mcs))
- Squeeze the bar hard during the lift. This will keep your entire body tight. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjz144njdfze6xjegvmcsw4))
- Before the descent, take another breath and go. Keep this air in until you’re about 2/3 of the way back up. Then you can let it out. I’ve taught myself to hold my breath for 3 reps, but this is very difficult and I wouldn’t recommend it for everyone. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzhv056ny69wzxap6fqmew))
- Bouncing out of the bottom position (the “hole”) is not a bad thing. Losing your air and tightness when you do so is. Don’t do that. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzjf1nc0cxfn1y2sfmnqt5))
- Your descent should be slow enough to permit you to maintain good form, but it should be fast enough to not waste energy or kill the stretch reflex at the bottom. Many lifters will “dive bomb” their squats. This is a fancy term for dropping very quickly and almost catching the bar in the bottom position before squatting back up. This is okay for advanced lifters who know their bodies and have great technique, but it’s probably unacceptable for about 99% of the rest of the population. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzk01etdjsgk9fcntwx26e))
- Your toes should be pointed out at about a 30-45 degree angle. This will allow your knees to track correctly. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzkz8we42v4d1pnra6wx3s))

### The Military Press

- Most lifters like taking a grip just outside the shoulders. I grip about an inch and a half outside the knurling of a power bar – which puts my grip about thumb’s length from the smooth part of the bar. This will ensure shoulder safety and stability. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzpez31xhn54tcf940ztwc))
- I always use a false grip where my thumbs aren’t wrapped around the bar. This is personal preference, but it seems like the bar path tracks better overhead when I do this. This grip can be dangerous, however, so use caution. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzq7a89tc4jfap9bkbe3sy))
- I begin the press by lifting my chin up, and I try to just miss it when the bar goes up. I don’t want the bar bowing out any more than it has to. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzr25rnzm3h6vp3rfykn7e))
- As the bar is pressed overhead, bring your head and chest through. Don’t push the bar back. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzs3vja3j3wzwycf8h1aqe))
- Bring the bar back down in the same motion. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzsvfbq8pgqwf0madk3nmv))
- Each rep should start with the bar on your shoulders. Don’t perform half reps. Your body was designed to move through a full range of motion, so do it. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzt3rvk16gpv6bvzhe068d))
- Keep your lower back arched and your chest up throughout the entire lift. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjztwrve1ka0vwsjn87745m))
- The positioning of your feet is determined by your comfort level. I take a narrow (less than shoulder width) stance, but this isn’t a requirement. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzv72qvkxp77w9gramkerx))
- You don’t use leg drive to push the bar overhead, but try to keep your legs strong and taut through the entire movement. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzvhx1q133n9e775anr2w9))

### The Bench Press

- Your shoulder blades must be pushed back, and your chest must be spread throughout the bench press. If you’re benching without the aid of a bench shirt, most of the bar weight (and your bodyweight) should be supported by your upper back. This will help keep the bar from touching too low. It will ensure that you don’t lose your tightness, and that you’ll be pressing from a strong bottom position. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzxr002zdm5mve2z7tqv39))
- Your lower back should be arched and kept that way. This does *not* mean you lift your butt off the bench. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxjzxxhxr9277051gff6sy54))
- Again, do NOT lift your butt off the bench. This bears repeating. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxk0jdcqwr8588ycjvd6srwa))
- Foot placement is up to you, but make sure they’re in a solid and strong position. I like to place my feet back slightly toward the head of the bench, and I assume a stance that’s narrow enough to allow my legs to squeeze the bench. - Grip width is also up to you. Over the years, I’ve found that a narrow grip is a little healthier in the long run than a wider grip. My grip is around 18” between index fingers. This may limit my weights in the short term, but it’ll ensure that I won’t have any pec or shoulder problems in the long run. It’s better to press today *and* tomorrow than just to press today. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpq2k827y9g425c3c1x1gy))
- As soon as the bar is in your hands, make sure your lower and upper back are arched, your feet are firmly on the floor, and your hands are wrapped tightly around the bar. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpqaf3rjzh3s0t60h32d1r))
- I like to take a large breath and force it into my diaphragm before lifting the bar off. This helps me feel stronger and more stable when I get the bar. A strong and easy lift-off is a great way to improve your lift and your mental state. Again, don’t “wimp” the bar off the rack. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpqzxw452b688pyctarqsx))
- Don’t use a lift-off partner when you train. Use this only for maximal attempts. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqprk7345dah62skv3p79mh))
- I usually hold my breath for the first 2-3 reps of every set. This is hard to do, but it ensures good technique and you won’t lose tightness. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqps1hnr52pgmyyv83rjh9m))
- If you’re using a closer grip like I do, the bar will touch higher on the body than it will with a wider grip – usually hitting just below my nipples. If you use a wider grip, the bar will hit slightly lower. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpsfkrbfy3x2etghdmajdm))
- Because you’ll be hitting higher on the chest with a closer grip, the bar will travel only slightly back toward your face. This is because you’ve already started the press closer to your head than you would have with a wider grip. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqptj9qawgjykppsmx6ghnm))
- With a wider grip, this path will be more pronounced because you’re hitting your chest lower. This will trace a “C” motion. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqptr315k9th8vbrd5revfq))
- • Keep your elbows tucked on the way down. This doesn’t mean they should be tucked into your sides. A slight tuck will suffice. If you tuck too hard, you’ll lose power off the bottom of the lift. This is where most people get stuck. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpv4sc16sp0053gym4jx1x))
- Once you touch your chest, keep your elbows tucked and drive the bar back slightly. About halfway up, allow your elbows to flare out, and keep pushing up and back. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jxqpw1se6xq0ny1y6b9c7g9z))
