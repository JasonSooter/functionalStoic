- *What does Bergson mean in this passage about cause/effect, rules of conduct, and the "creative principle" enabling love of humanity?*
  Bergson is saying moral power doesn’t spring from abstract truths, causal proofs, or mere rules; it comes from a lived contact with humanity’s creative life‑force (élan vital). Rules are useful scaffolding but not the source of moral energy. Genuine love of humanity arises when one participates in the inventive, sustaining current of human life rather than treating morality as a detached proposition. - *How does Bergson’s account of morality contrast with Kant’s, and why does that matter if Im moving to Germany?*
  Kant grounds morality in reason, duty, and universalizable rules (the Categorical Imperative); rules and motive from duty are central. Bergson grounds morality in life’s creative impulse: an embodied, future‑oriented vitality that yields responsibility. For Germany: social institutions and everyday norms often operate Kantianly—procedural, rule‑driven fairness—so it helps to behave Kantian outwardly (follow rules) while keeping Bergsonian vitality internally (prioritize lived care and creativity). - *Why is this Bergson passage important in Bonjour Tristesse when Cécile reads it in her study of philosophy?*

The quote highlights her deficit—not maliciousness but moral non‑contact: she studies ethics intellectually without the sustaining sympathy or responsibility that constitute moral strength. - *How do Anne and Raymond fit (or fail to fit) Bergsons idea of moral vitality in Bonjour Tristesse?*
  Anne embodies Bergson’s ideal: she channels life into continuity, responsibility, and care—moral strength lived, not preached. Raymond has the élan vital—sensuality, warmth, energy—but refuses to bind it to duration or responsibility, so his vitality is undisciplined. The tragedy arises when Raymond aligns with Cécile’s detached cleverness: vitality allied with irresponsibility becomes destructive rather than sustaining life.
