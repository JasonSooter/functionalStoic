## Highlights
- *If I move to Germany with a job at Twilio, how long must I be employed/residing in Germany to obtain permanent residence for myself, my wife, and my three children?*
  Germany’s “permanent residence” is usually the Settlement Permit (Niederlassungserlaubnis). If you enter on an EU Blue Card you can qualify fastest: after 21 months of qualifying employment and pension contributions with German at B1, or after 27 months with basic German (A1). On a standard work permit the usual route is about 5 years (including 60 months of pension contributions). Your wife can join immediately; she typically follows the 5‑year rule (in some cases 3 years if you already hold a settlement permit and you live together). Children can only obtain their own settlement permit at age 16 if they have already held a residence permit for 5 years by their 16th birthday. To preserve the fastest path, use a Blue Card/skilled‑worker arrangement rather than a temporary ICT assignment. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/69516cca-f900-8327-82c4-9c81e6111ccc))
- *If my 13‑year‑old won’t get a settlement permit until 18/19, will she be limited — for example, can she still attend school and go to university in Germany?*
  As your dependent child she will have a residence permit on family grounds and can attend German schools and later apply to universities as long as you keep legal residence. University admission depends on academic/qualification and language requirements (Abitur or recognized equivalent, German or English tests), not on having a settlement permit. However, some state benefits (e.g., BAföG, certain scholarships) and independent residency rights are linked to long‑term/settlement status. The earlier 16‑year settlement shortcut only applies if she has already held a residence permit for 5 years by age 16, so it likely won’t apply if you arrive when she’s 13. Check BAföG and scholarship eligibility and keep her residence permit renewed. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/69516cca-f900-8327-82c4-9c81e6111ccc))
- *Can you map a timeline for my 13‑year‑old daughter (currently in 7th grade) after we move to Germany — schooling, residency/settlement permit timing, and university transition?*
  If you move when she’s 13 (7th grade):
  Year 0 (arrival): she gets a family‑reunification residence permit and enrolls in German school; renew permits as required.
  Years 0–5 (ages ~13–18): attend school (aim for Gymnasium/Abitur or equivalent). After five years of continuous lawful residence (around age 18) she will normally qualify to apply for a settlement permit.
  At 18 she must hold her own residence title — typically a student permit if she starts university. Getting a German Abitur helps university entry and can exempt her from non‑EU tuition in Baden‑Württemberg; student permits can affect BAföG eligibility, so keep renewals and documentation continuous. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/69516cca-f900-8327-82c4-9c81e6111ccc))
- *If my daughter doesn't go to university, how will her schooling/vocational pathways and residency/settlement timeline proceed after arriving at 13 — and how does entering Gymnasium in grade 7 (when Gymnasium usually starts in grades 5/6) affect that?*
  She can finish lower secondary school and then enter vocational training (Berufsschule plus an apprenticeship/Ausbildung), commonly beginning around age ~16 after a Haupt‑ or Realschulabschluss. Your daughter’s residence permit can be kept for training or work; continuous lawful residence from arrival counts toward the 5‑year clock. Arriving at 13 gives her ~5 years by age 18, so she could be eligible for permanent residence around 18 if she has sufficient German and is in employment/training or has financial means. Starting Gymnasium in grade 7 mainly affects whether she pursues Abitur (university track) versus earlier school‑leaving certificates, but it doesn’t prevent vocational routes — just keep her permit renewals continuous and monitor required certificates for specific apprenticeships. - *Will the 2nd foreign language be "English" with German + English being the 2 languages?*
  No. In Baden-Württemberg German does not count as one of the two "Fremdsprachen." Gymnasium requires two foreign languages in addition to German. Typically English is the 1st foreign language (from Grade 5) and the 2nd must be a different language (often French or Latin, from Grade 6).
  English can only be the 2nd if it isn’t already the 1st at that school, and in some cases a heritage language may substitute subject to school/authority approval. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/69516cca-f900-8327-82c4-9c81e6111ccc))
