## Highlights
- The good news: coffee isn’t *killing* us. Epidemiological studies show that people who drink two to four cups a day actually have a lower risk of early death than non-drinkers ([Sundhed.dk](https://www.sundhed.dk/borger/patienthaandbogen/mave-tarm/tilstande/kaffe/)). ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cnbgwm5tfayceqkw5crt6b))
- Coffee appears to reduce the risk of certain diseases - though, intriguingly, the benefits show up with both caffeinated and decaffeinated versions. So, it’s not the caffeine that’s saving us, but possibly the antioxidants. Or just the joy of a mug of something comforting. Or the ritual of grinding, brewing and sipping that acts as a pep talk in a cup ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cnd87mt94eqkjrvna0t2py))
- (though I can’t help feeling this might be a lot like the red wine ‘evidence’ – where people who habitually drink it tended to be better off, with more resources to take care of themselves and so healthier). ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cndvjc9t6x5dfz4pcv25e1))
- But there’s something very *Danishly* pragmatic about Brendborg’s message. It fits with a stoic Nordic acceptance of reality: there’s no magic bullet, no “energy in a cup,” only *hygge* pleasures and self-discipline. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cngejm7e0kq4ksazavq50b))
- The Scandinavian secret to surviving winter isn’t superfood smoothies or early morning runs; it’s acknowledging that we’re all tired, then lighting a candle and drinking coffee anyway. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cnhkak1hfeadwkdf00cfr8))
- In Denmark, “Skal vi tage en kop kaffe?” (“Shall we have a coffee?”) is shorthand for *let’s pause and be human for a bit*. And that’s an energy we all need more of - not from caffeine, but from connection. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8cnkfg2a9ba05y5y917aras))
