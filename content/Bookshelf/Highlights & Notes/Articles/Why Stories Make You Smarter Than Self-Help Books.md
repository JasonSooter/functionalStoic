- They follow a template: introduce a counterintuitive finding, tell three anecdotes that illustrate it, mention some studies (p < 0.05, naturally), provide a framework with a memorable acronym, conclude with actionable advice. Stretch this to 250 pages, add some graphs, and you have a bestseller. The information density is incredibly low. You're not learning complex systems of thought; you're learning to repeat interesting-sounding facts. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabn4r64xeqpjvdhrnvxr0k7))
- Fiction (by contrast) smuggles actual complexity into your brain. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabn5a6v60gtj33c01ye00qj))
- When Dostoevsky spends fifty pages letting Raskolnikov justify murder to himself, you're not learning about moral philosophy in the abstract. You're living inside a mind that's trying to reason its way to atrocity. You understand something about human rationalization that no Gladwell volume could teach you. The knowledge comes embedded in context, emotion, and contradiction. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabn5p07cyf51jssmqs8kckm))
- I suspect this is why the smartest people I know tend to quote novels more than they quote non-fiction. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabn88x5s72pty2f1hp62ze0))

- I used to think I was being practical by reading mostly non-fiction. I was learning things! Accumulating facts! Becoming informed about psychology, economics, history, science. But the conversations that lingered were with people who read novels. They have a different kind of intelligence, more contextual and subtle. They understand human nature in a way that knowing cold facts about cognitive biases never quite captures. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabnmye0bhg8wme0zdkja4gg))

- The problem with self-help books is the assumption that wisdom can be systematized and imparted through instruction. But wisdom resists systematization. It's pattern recognition across too many variables to count. It's knowing when rules apply and when they don't. Fiction trains this capacity by forcing you to navigate moral and social complexity without clear answers. There's no "key takeaways" section because life doesn't have key takeaways. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01kabnqbcznrjkzg4fksfrryyj))
