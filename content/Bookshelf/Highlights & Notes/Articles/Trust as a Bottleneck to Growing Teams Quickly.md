## Highlights

- Trust comes from [common knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_knowledge_%5c%28logic%5c%29) of shared mental models, and that takes investment from both sides to build. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvsgvt4zqfbzf5ay23nm3se6))
- If low trust is sometimes appropriate, what’s the problem? It’s that *trust is what lets collaboration scale.* ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvsgweyamnfrqmbnb7azka3n))
- Some ways to invest more effort in trusting others that I’ve seen work well: ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvsha2q16s8mvxn4w07nssx9))
- Share your most important mental models broadly. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshabrkrn9prm0krsy3j8ad))
- At Anthropic, Dario gives biweekly-ish “informal vision updates” (hour-long talks on important updates to parts of company strategy) that I think of as the canonical example of this. Just about everyone at Anthropic is trying to build an internal “Dario simulator” who they can consult when the real one is too busy (i.e. ~always). For high level strategy, these updates do an amazing job of that. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshap9m2d8h119wd9nzt84g))

- Put in time. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshek88a404kgmbws3s2c44))
- In addition to one-way broadcasts, trust-building benefits a lot from one-on-one bidirectional communication so that you can get feedback on how well the other person is building the right models. This is one of the reasons I schedule lots of recurring 1:1s with peers in addition to my team. Offsites are also very helpful here. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshexpgeq5zyjjvb34dk8z1))

> My 1:1 w/ Clarissa is an example of this

- Try people out. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshg4rd5nywnmf2d3d3wfer))
- If you’re unsure whether someone on your team will be great at something, try giving them a trial task and monitoring how it’s going more closely than you would by default, to catch issues early. This is a great way to invest in your long-term ability to delegate things. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshgc8rqbkax4t6gjedn76z))

> We should take this strategy for SAs that might be added to JPMC

- Give feedback ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshkfmhdfqpjtf452rsj7vq))
- It’s easy to feel like something is “too minor” to give feedback on and let it slide, especially when there’s always too much to do. But I’ve never regretted erring on the side of giving feedback, and often regretted deciding to “deal with it” or keep quiet. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshm8qg0qwgd37t8bpnpv97))

> The feedback about improving Segment Product Updates is a good example

- One pro-tip here: if you feel anxious about giving someone negative feedback, consider whether you’ve given them enough *positive feedback*—which is a helpful buffer against people interpreting negative feedback as “you’re not doing well overall.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshp0kzzw167ecembz03hsp))

> How could I have utilized this technique with my Segment Product Update feedback?

- To help other people trust you: ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshsmy6dy39eyvd8yb5h2px))
- Accept that you start out with incomplete trust. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshsxb4d48hnp8q8g96fah1))
- When someone, say, tries to monitor my work more closely than I think is warranted, my initial reaction is to be defensive and ask them to trust me more. It takes effort to put myself into their shoes and remind myself that they probably don’t have a good enough model of me to trust me yet. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshtb8jpktsnnd2g7t5cn4c))
- Overcommunicate status ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshtetn6nxfe9bjhrtd1smd))
- This helps in two ways: first, it gives stakeholders more confidence that if something goes off the rails they’ll know quickly. And second, it gives them more data and helps them build a higher-fidelity model of how you operate. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshtwdbndd5sn43hb59jg9w))
- **Proactively own up** when something isn’t going wel ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jvshv1dtq45t322vcxh8wnnv))
- Arguably a special case of overcommunicating, but one that’s especially important to get right: if you can be relied on to ask for help when you need it, it’s a lot less risky for people to “try you out” on stuff at the edge of what they trust you on.
