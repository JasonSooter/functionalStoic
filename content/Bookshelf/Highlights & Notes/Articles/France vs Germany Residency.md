## Highlights
- *Compare residency requirements of France vs Germany for a US citizen. Can I move to France without having employment?*
  France: Yes — you can get a long-stay “visiteur” (VLS‑TS) to stay >3 months without working, but you must validate it online within 3 months, show health insurance, accommodation, and sufficient funds (practical benchmark ~€1,426.30 net/month/person). No professional activity is allowed (including risky remote work).
  Germany: No general “no work” lane; residence titles must state a purpose. Germany has a job‑search route (Opportunity Card) but most non‑work stays (study, family reunification) are purpose‑bound. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/693c7610-7f34-8326-b1d5-169ecb5bed49))
- *Would moving to France (near Freiburg) be a good stepping-stone to apply for German jobs while living in Fort Collins?*
  France can be a useful logistical base (same time zone, nearby for in‑person interviews), but it does not grant German work rights. You’d still need a German residence/work permit to be employed in Germany. For job searching in Germany, the Opportunity Card or being physically in Germany on the correct permit is a more direct/legal route; France helps interviews and travel, not legal eligibility. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/693c7610-7f34-8326-b1d5-169ecb5bed49))
- *Family must come along and I can fund €1,426/person/month for 1 year (x5). Is that enough to base the family in France as visitors while job-searching Germany?*
  That €1,426.30 figure is in euros and is the common per‑person benchmark used for a French visitor permit. For five people it totals about €85,578/year. With that funding plus health insurance and rental proof, a family can qualify for France’s long‑stay “visiteur” route — but note: no professional activity allowed and Schengen 90/180 rules limit time spent in other Schengen states (so you can’t live in Germany from a French visa). - *Would my children be able to attend school in France for that year?*
  Yes. Education is compulsory in France for resident children ages 3–16 and public schools are free. You typically register via the mairie, then complete enrollment at the assigned school. Schools usually ask for proof of address, ID/birth certificates and vaccination records. Newly arrived non‑French speakers are integrated into mainstream classes with language support services. ([View Highlight](https://chatgpt.com/c/693c7610-7f34-8326-b1d5-169ecb5bed49))
- *Can you homeschool in France for that year (instead of enrolling in public school)?*
  Homeschooling (Instruction en famille) is legally possible but tightly controlled: since 2022 it requires prior annual authorization and is allowed only for specified reasons (health, itinerant families, high‑level sport/arts, distance, etc.). Enrolling in CNED’s full regulated program for under‑16s also requires that authorization. So homeschooling is not a default alternative — public school is the straightforward option. - *Living costs in Strasbourg (family of 5). Non‑French‑speaking support in schools. Is Strasbourg bike/pedestrian/transit friendly (can we be car‑free)?*
  Rent is the biggest variable: median rent ~€11.1/m²/month; a 90–110 m² T4/T5 typically aligns with ~€1,000–€1,330/month + charges as a baseline. Public transit is robust (single ride ~€1.90, 24h €4.60, cross‑border monthly ~€70); Vélhop annual bikes ~€54. Schools: Académie de Strasbourg offers CASNAV/UPE2A language integration — kids enter mainstream classes with targeted French support. Strasbourg is highly bike‑ and pedestrian‑friendly (600+ km cycling network) and well‑served by trams, so a car is optional if you choose housing near stops.
