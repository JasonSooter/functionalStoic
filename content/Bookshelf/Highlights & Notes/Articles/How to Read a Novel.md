- I sketched out an answer to this question in my book *[Farsighted](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/309724/farsighted-by-steven-johnson/)*, arguing that novels (and fictional narratives in general) were extensions of the human mind’s marvelous aptitude for building *simulations* of potential events. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jme6wj8tyfr5vk1psjkd46ry))
	- Tags: #add-book-to-read
- Narratives of all sorts allow you to parachute into other simulated experiences, which ultimately give you more data for your own simulations. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jme6xt0ttfhe7tvjnm5rvr4r))
- novels, I would argue, give you the richest simulation of the *interior life* of other people’s experiences: you get a ringside view of all that emotional and cognitive action. This is particularly true of the novel after, say, 1750 or so, when the novelists began adopting more of the inner monologues (both first-person and “close-third” perspective) that Shakespeare had explored on the stage. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jme6yn8f9mbry3ce18zq9hns))
	- [ ] Add this point to a section on reading novels. This reasoning aligns well with what I strive for
- It seems fairly obvious to me that there is practical utility in running these simulations. We accumulate wisdom that we can apply to our own lives by watching other people live theirs. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jme719rgbfgjphqhga174nff))
- Historical nonfiction—particularly if the author has access to the subject’s inner life through journals or correspondence—arguably has even more utility, in that the events in question actually happened in the real world, and not in the imagination of the novelist. (This is one reason I decided to write nonfiction instead of novels—the other being that I don’t think I’m a good enough writer to write novels.) ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jme71shaxkz54fff6413sqvq))
- Both novels succeed at something else: they invite us to think across multiple scales of experience—and to make causal and associative connections between those different layers. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk0eaq2377d0envs3vjykf7))

	- [ ] Connect with Orientalism
	 Edward Said: A prominent Palestinian-American scholar, literary critic, and public intellectual, Edward Said (1935–2003) is best known for his influential work in postcolonial studies and his critique of Western representations of the East. His seminal book, *Orientalism* (1978), argues that Western literature and scholarship have historically constructed an image of the East as the "Other," serving to justify colonial domination. Said's insights into the interplay between culture and power have had a profound impact on various fields, including literature, cultural studies, and political theory, prompting critical re-evaluations of imperial narratives in Western literature. His advocacy for Palestinian rights and his critiques of U.S. foreign policy further established him as a significant voice in contemporary discourse on identity, culture, and empire.

- Back to Collison’s original question: why should we read these books? ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk15bbw5ta81hvhz3znyykd))
- To me, the answer comes down to this: getting your brain to accurately assess the full spectrum takes effort, but it’s a valuable skill to have in life. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk15g0knz4qd7kr6zhqvt28))
- If you over-index on one layer at the expense of the others, you’ll end up making less nuanced choices, both personally and professionally, because your mental model about what is *actually happening* at a given crossroads of your life will be too narrow. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk16tymnn3ndazrkyjkhbnf))
- And so we should embrace any opportunity to practice thinking across scales, in part because most educational environments are deliberately designed to keep you specialized in one part of the spectrum. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk17dg065tv8dj03pv0s2nq))
- So in a way, I’ve come to think about books like *Middlemarch* or *Bleak House*—using the language of deep learning—as a source of training data for full-spectrum thinking. They are trial runs that prepare you for the real thing. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk1831m7b7k15j1r5ky330a))
- There are other reasons to read novels, of course; ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk18wnyw50vw0j0xmrfyhbf))
- both *Middlemarch* and *Bleak House* have beautifully crafted plots that are genuinely fun to unravel, if you can keep track of the characters, and there are some legendary sentences in each book. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk19ffv2kzfggpzykm3gkvj))
- Another reason to read them—which Collison emphasizes as well—is that they are extremely effective vehicles for time-traveling back to earlier periods in history. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk19xkx18b5nw4v2htvt0jd))
- There is, I think, something intrinsically valuable in having that glimpse of humanity in a slightly different configuration—different values, technologies, economic structures—that widens your perception of your own moment. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jmk1ad15tgqm0q9eq90j014x))
