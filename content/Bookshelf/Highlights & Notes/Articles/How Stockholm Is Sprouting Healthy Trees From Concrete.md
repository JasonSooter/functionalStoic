## Highlights

- When Stockholm’s Traffic Office conducted a general assessment of street traffic in the Swedish capital in 2001, it came to the shocking conclusion that two-thirds of all trees in the city center were dead or dying.
	City authorities agreed that an urgent response was needed to nurse these leafy urban ecosystem pillars back to health. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0qm1s8gmc29ppjnnactxy))
- Enter Björn Embrén, Stockholm’s first “tree officer.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0mabk3mt70dedkhh0kzhe))

> Such an awesome title! Of course Scandinavian countries would have a Tree Officer. This reminds me of the section in Walkable City that discussed the value of trees. With Portland being the primary example of the value put on trees

- The root of the problem was the fact that many trees in Stockholm were planted in the 19th century. Back then, the streets were comparatively underdeveloped, so the trees could grow relatively freely in the space underground. But as Stockholm has developed over the decades, like many cities across the world, its streets have been increasingly paved over with concrete, and more pipes — for plumbing, electricity, internet and beyond — have been laid underneath them. As a consequence, the roots of the trees have often been damaged through disruptive building works and, due to the lack of space and impermeable concrete, they have also been starved of oxygen and water. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0tt2cd14jc5jypb6htrze))
- Eventually, inspired by railroad embankments — which require only a small amount of organic matter for healthy trees to grow — Embrén concocted what was to become known as the “Stockholm tree pit” model. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0vntqe8ewtv2487jh1j8g))
	- [ ] Investigate the Stockholm Tree Pit. How does it work and what does it look like?
- The design involves digging a pit and constructing a frame underground around the tree’s roots, and then filling said pit with a mixture of soil and stone, sometimes including [biochar](https://reasonstobecheerful.world/netzero-coffee-husks-biochar-climate-reduce-emissions/), to both aerate and fertilize the soil. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0w7fprkjmax9f1v5xfsec))
- These permeable layers are very strong and physically adaptable but also allow stormwater to flow in, meaning the trees are provided with sufficient air and water naturally. They also allow rainwater to be soaked up — a necessity amid more extreme weather brought on by climate change. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg0wxe43tgc03drfa1hndw0))
- According to the [United Nations Economic Commission for Europe](https://unece.org/media/news/366653), urban forests can help “future-proof” cities, which are responsible for about 75 percent of global CO2 emissions. Sustainable urban forestry, it says, can bring multiple benefits, such as lowering temperatures, improving public health, creating habitats for biodiversity, sequestering carbon, generating green jobs, and mitigating risks of floods and landslides. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jpg195dv990s11fs7rydjhqs))
