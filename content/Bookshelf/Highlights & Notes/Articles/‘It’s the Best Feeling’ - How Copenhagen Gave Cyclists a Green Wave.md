## Highlights
- Traffic lights are synchronised so a rush-hour cyclist at 20km/h can catch green lights all the way
  “Hitting a green wave? It’s the best feeling, especially when you’re in a hurry,” says actor Samuel Traum, unlocking his bike by a Copenhagen intersection thronged by two-wheeled commuters. “The opposite, a red wave, is the worst.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f6z1nsbzcz04qgtq53qrac))
- For those of us who do not live in cycle-friendly Copenhagen, the green wave that Traum describes is a system started by the city authorities 16 years ago: in order to encourage bike travel, they synchronised traffic lights on several key roads so that a rush-hour cyclist travelling at about 20km/h (12.4mph) could catch green lights all the way. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f6zvd2wwgx5ns9qvdsp8jq))
- Green waves are now set to spread to 15 more routes after the municipality approved new cycling provisions in the budget this month. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f70fz5rtcc6kp69kj5eg4x))
- Like many cities in Europe, Copenhagen became a city of cars after the second world war. In the 1970s, however, it began to fight back against the traffic with huge bike protests that pushed the municipality to build a vast network of cycle lanes. If every city had as many, a [study](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2422334122) found in June, the total carbon dioxide emissions from private vehicles would fall by 6%. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f720em2my7v3rr1qv8qg6a))
- But officials admit that even Copenhagen is struggling to cut car use among those who travel into the city from outside. The construction of cycle superhighways has helped commuters reach the outskirts by bike, but red lights slow them down when they reach the streets. It is here that transport planners hope green waves will help. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f72qv728t50wp3njhssqxr))
- “It’s so important to have the freedom on a bike to just ride along at a good speed,” says Line Barfod, the mayor for technology and environment, who zips down Nørrebrogade every day on her way to city hall and argues that more green waves will encourage people to ditch their cars. “You can feel the city wants you – and supports you – going on a bike.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f73pnm3mne29v0z19pvx5h))
- The expansion of the traffic light tweak has faced little political pushback and residents who have experienced green waves speak glowingly of them. Barfod says: “The war between cars and bikes is more on parking spaces than changing speeds.” ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01k8f74snptxxc7k94g08x6cyp))
