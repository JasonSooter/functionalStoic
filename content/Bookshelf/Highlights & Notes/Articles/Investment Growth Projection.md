  Rough growth scenarios for $1,000,000 left untouched:

  - 5% p.a.: ~ $1.63M (10y), ~$2.65M (20y).
  - 7% p.a.: ~ $1.97M (10y), ~$3.87M (20y).
  To prepare: factor in inflation, taxes and currency effects if you move to Germany; set an emergency cash buffer; choose an age-appropriate asset allocation and gradually de-risk toward retirement; plan income (safe-withdrawal ~3–4% as a starting guide), consider annuities/pensions, healthcare and long-term-care costs, and estate/tax planning. - *What should I expect from Germany's retirement system if I become a German resident?*
  As a German resident you'll primarily rely on the statutory pay‑as‑you‑go pension (Deutsche Rentenversicherung). Employees and employers jointly contribute roughly 18–19% of gross wages; benefits are credited as “pension points” based on your earnings relative to the national average. Full pension age is being phased to about 67 for younger cohorts and you must meet minimum contribution periods to qualify. Self‑employed and civil servants use different arrangements; many Germans also rely on occupational (bAV) and private pensions to top up statutory benefits. Statutory pensions are taxable and subject to health/long‑term‑care contributions, which reduce net payouts. EU rules and bilateral agreements (e.g., US‑Germany) let you combine contribution periods and generally allow portability. Practical steps: register with German social security, request your Versicherungsverlauf (insurance record) from the DRV, get a pension projection, and consult a cross‑border pension/tax adviser to model effects of contributions, taxes and healthcare.
