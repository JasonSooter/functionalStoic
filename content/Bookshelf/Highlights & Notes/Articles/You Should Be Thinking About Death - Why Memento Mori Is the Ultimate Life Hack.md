## Highlights

- Tim Ferriss observed:

	> *"I think about death all the time and it's not a morbid, sullen exercise for me… I find it to be, and this might sound strange, but greatly encouraging because it drives a sense of urgency, or at least time sensitivity, to a lot of my decisions."* ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh04n1ma4yva5ke8w29vdx2))
	- [ ] Add to a note "On Death"

- Ryan Holiday puts it even more directly in his exploration of Stoic practices:
	"Meditating on your mortality is only depressing if you miss the point. It is in fact a tool to create priority and meaning." ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0674382kq2pvwzsc0fmw3))
- Emperor Marcus Aurelius reminded himself, "You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think." ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh06xy61yfjp2w737j6dm6q))
- The irony is that befriending death doesn't make life feel shorter or scarier. It makes it feel more vivid, more precious, more worth living authentically rather than performatively. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0acsepypyn81hzmyqj5nn))
- When you truly internalize that you could leave life right now—not as some abstract philosophy but as lived reality—several things happen: ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0b2w694j9czrtzen7yzrb))
- **Your real values emerge from the noise.** Suddenly, being seen as successful matters less than actually connecting with people you love. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0b76qw09rkg8fb779f5b4))
- **Fake urgency reveals itself.** That "ASAP" email? Unless someone's actually dying, it can wait. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0b9bm9fnyd2npkvd4zm4x))
- **Your tolerance for bullshit approaches zero.** Life's too short for meetings that should have been emails or relationships that drain more than they give. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0berd7092fjhc62xdzyf4))
- **What actually matters becomes blindingly clear.** Hint: It's usually much simpler than your brain wants to believe. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0bp5bp1ztk4djbt02a24n))

### Practical Memento Mori: Tools for the Living

- a few more concrete practices that bring death's clarity into daily life: ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0zaw99hg674m8d1tx7evh))
- Write Your Own Eulogy ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh0zmhnwqr5pjn4n0cbdday))
- I recommend writing two versions: ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh105a72vxhpdz3xetdg8t9))
- Write the eulogy for if you died today, then write the one you'd want read if you lived aligned with what truly matters. The gap between them is the work for you to do and the places for you to focus. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh10a09g8newxsb0tvrm6qs))
- The Deathbed Story Filter ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh1130yzf1ma2z7w7f1qryc))
- Before any major decision, ask: "On my deathbed, will I regret not doing this, or will I regret the things I sacrificed to do it? What’s the story I wish to be able to tell about this when I’m dying?" This question has helped me see through superfacial achievement traps and, on the other side, has helped me choose the short-term painful thing that benefits me in the long-term. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh11k4pz0kz5c9e201b1hmf))
- **Study the Stars and Get Outside** Adapting Ferriss's advice, go outside at night and look up. Find a star. Consider that its light traveled years to reach you, that the star itself might already be gone. Find ways to be in grand scenes in nature. Find places that bring you ‘awe’. Let that cosmic perspective shrink your problems to their actual size. ([View Highlight](https://read.readwise.io/read/01jyh122np6ysdxxbp44jdee2s))
