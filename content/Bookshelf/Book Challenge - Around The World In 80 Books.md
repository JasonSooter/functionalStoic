# Around the World in 80 Books V1

This list of 80 books (one per country) approximates Phileas Fogg’s eastward route in *Around the World in 80 Days*, starting and ending in London, but with less emphasis on war. It covers daily life, culture, social change, identity, folklore, family, and personal growth—available in English (originally or in translation).

---

## Segment 1: Europe (Starting in London and Moving east)

1. **England**
	 - *English Passengers* by Matthew Kneale
	 - *Why:* Captures 19th-century English character and maritime exploration.

2. **France**
	 - *The Elegance of the Hedgehog* by Muriel Barbery
	 - *Why:* Modern French intellectual life, set in a Paris apartment building.

3. **Belgium**
	 - *Madame Verona Comes Down the Hill* by Dimitri Verhulst
	 - *Why:* Quiet, affectionate look at rural Belgian village life.

4. **Netherlands**
	 - *The Tea Lords* by Hella S. Haasse
	 - *Why:* Reflects the Netherlands’ colonial past and Dutch connections with the East Indies.

5. **Germany**
	 - *Measuring the World* by Daniel Kehlmann
	 - *Why:* A playful historical novel about Humboldt and Gauss, celebrating German science and exploration.

6. **Czech Republic**
	 - *Prague Tales* by Jan Neruda
	 - *Why:* Classic Czech short stories capturing 19th-century Prague’s life and atmosphere.

7. **Austria**
	 - *A Whole Life* by Robert Seethaler
	 - *Why:* A succinct, moving portrait of a man’s simple life in the Austrian Alps.

8. **Hungary**
	 - *The Door* by Magda Szabó
	 - *Why:* Explores friendship, secrets, and social shifts in post-war Hungary.

9. **Slovakia**
	 - *The Equestrienne* by Uršuľa Kovalyk
	 - *Why:* Contemporary Slovak novel following a woman’s dream of escape via equestrian circus arts.

10. **Poland**
	 - *Flights* by Olga Tokarczuk
	 - *Why:* Philosophical reflections on [[travel]], identity, and Europe’s fluid borders.

# Around the World in 80 Literary Classics V2

Below is a curated list of 80 books, each representing a different country, chosen for their status as **canonical** or **classical** works—akin to the cultural and literary importance of *Moby-Dick*. The route follows a rough eastward path, starting and ending in England, much like Phileas Fogg in *Around the World in 80 Days.* Some countries (especially smaller or postcolonial ones) have more modern or less universally known works due to limited English translations of older classics.

> **Note:**
> - “Classic” varies by culture and academic tradition.
> - For some countries, no single “Moby-Dick-level” novel is available in English, so a 20th-century or modern classic is chosen.
> - This list involves compromises where local canons don’t align with older, widely translated texts.

---

## Segment 1: Western Europe (Start in England)

1. **England**
	 - **Book:** *[[Middlemarch]]* by George Eliot (1871–72)
	 - **Why:** Often cited as one of the greatest English novels, on par with *Moby-Dick* in scope.

2. **France**
	 - **Book:** *Les Misérables* by Victor Hugo (1862)
	 - **Why:** One of the most famous 19th-century French novels, central to the French canon.

3. **Belgium**
	 - **Book:** *Bruges-la-Morte* by Georges Rodenbach (1892)
	 - **Why:** A Symbolist classic set in Bruges, influential in Belgian literature.

4. **Netherlands**
	 - **Book:** *Max Havelaar* by Multatuli (1860)
	 - **Why:** Seminal Dutch novel indicting colonial abuses; cornerstone of Dutch literary history.

5. **Germany**
	 - **Book:** *Faust* (Part I) by Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1808)
	 - **Why:** Goethe’s masterpiece, foundational in the German literary canon.

6. **Czech Republic**
	 - **Book:** *The Good Soldier Švejk* by Jaroslav Hašek (1921–23)
	 - **Why:** A satirical classic, pivotal in Czech literature and widely influential.

7. **Austria**
	 - **Book:** *The Radetzky March* by Joseph Roth (1932)
	 - **Why:** Chronicles the decline of the Austro-Hungarian Empire; revered Austrian classic.

8. **Hungary**
	 - **Book:** *Embers* by Sándor Márai (1942)
	 - **Why:** Modern Hungarian classic rediscovered globally; explores friendship and memory.

9. **Slovakia**
	 - **Book:** *Jozef Mak* by Jozef Cíger Hronský (1934)
	 - **Why:** An important 20th-century Slovak novel portraying rural life and personal fate.

10. **Poland**
	 - **Book:** *Pan Tadeusz* by Adam Mickiewicz (1834)
	 - **Why:** Poland’s national epic poem in verse; a monumental pillar of Polish literature.
